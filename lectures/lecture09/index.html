1 · The paper

Which multivariate GARCH model forecasts best?

Portfolio choice, hedging and value-at-risk all need a forecast of the conditional covariance matrix \(H_t\) of many assets at once. Dozens of multivariate GARCH (MGARCH) models exist, but, as the authors put it, "from an applied viewpoint there are no clear guidelines available on model evaluation and selection".

The experiment (Section 2 and Table I): 10 liquid NYSE stocks · 125 MGARCH specifications (105 conditional-correlation, 17 orthogonal, 3 BEKK-type) · forecast horizons of 1, 5 and 20 days · 6 realised-covariance proxies · 4 statistical loss functions · two multiple-comparison tests, the model confidence set (MCS) and the superior predictive ability (SPA) test.

The forecast sample runs over 10 years (2,486 trading days, 1 April 1999 to 27 December 2008), split into three volatility regimes: the dot-com bubble and burst, a calm period, and the 2007-08 financial crisis.

The data (Section 3, Table II)

1-minute prices from TickData's OMED database, 2 March 1988 to 27 December 2008; after cleaning, 5,226 trading days. Models are estimated on daily open-to-close returns; forecasts are scored against realised covariance from 5-minute intraday returns. Headline findings: full sample (Table III)

  • Under the symmetric Euclidean loss the MCS keeps 39 of 125 models - dominated by orthogonal and DECO specifications; the best sample performer is Orth.-GJR(2,1) (average loss 3.468)
  • Under the asymmetric Stein loss (under-predictions punished) the MCS shrinks to 10 models, all DCC-family, led by integrated and long-memory variances
  • Under the \(L_3\) loss (over-predictions punished) 20 models survive, mostly orthogonal - a hint that orthogonal models under-predict the covariance matrix
  • Models with leverage (GJR, EGARCH, APARCH variances) systematically outperform symmetric ones, and higher-order lags beat single lags on sample loss, though differences are often not significant
Key diagnosis: full-sample results are "strongly driven by short periods of high market instability during which multivariate GARCH models appear to be inaccurate" - the average Euclidean loss is about 0.3 in the calm period but 14.5 during the 2007-08 crisis.

Headline findings: regimes, horizons, benchmark

  • Dot-com turbulence: the MCS (38 models under \(L_E\)) is dominated by DCC-type models with EGARCH leverage in the variances; under Stein loss only two models survive (DCCE and DCCT with IGARCH variances)
  • Calm markets: 74 of 125 models survive - "a simple assumption like constant conditional correlation and symmetry in the conditional variances cannot be rejected"
  • 2007-08 crisis: non-stationary variances (IGARCH, FIGARCH, RiskMetrics) generate superior forecasts; EGARCH specifications are systematically rejected
  • Longer horizons (Table IV): at 5 and 20 days the MCS shrinks to 16 and 12 models; explosive non-stationary forecasts are rejected and RiskMetrics multi-step forecasts are "uninformative" (flat at the 1-step value)
  • SPA test (Table V): the DCC of Engle (2002) with leverage (EGARCH) in the variances is never significantly outperformed by any of the other 124 models, whatever the period - the paper's practical recommendation

2 · Preparing the data

Open-to-close, not close-to-close

Open-to-close returns (used here)
$$r_t = 100\,(\ln P_t^{close} - \ln P_t^{open})$$
Measure volatility while the market is open - the same hours the 5-minute realised-covariance proxy sees. Model forecast and proxy then measure the same thing.
Close-to-close returns
$$r_t = 100\,(\ln P_t^{close} - \ln P_{t-1}^{close})$$
Include the overnight move, which intraday data cannot see. Following Andersen et al. (2010) the paper treats overnight returns as deterministic jumps and strips them out.
  • Zero-return days: in the estimation sample 6% to 11.5% of daily returns are exactly zero (stale or non-traded days, early years especially) - a reason the authors picked the most liquid NYSE names
  • Scale: returns are in percent; on a raw scale \(\omega\) would be about \(10^{-4}\) smaller and optimisers struggle
  • Demeaning: the conditional mean is "of minor importance" for variance forecasting - the paper subtracts each stock's unconditional mean and models \( \varepsilon_t = r_t - \bar r \) directly

Variance targeting: fewer free parameters

MGARCH likelihoods have many parameters and nasty surfaces. Variance targeting (Engle and Mezrich, 1995) pins the model's long-run covariance to the sample moment instead of estimating it:

$$\bar H = \frac{1}{T}\sum_t \varepsilon_t\varepsilon_t' \qquad\Rightarrow\qquad C = \bar H - A\bar H A' - B\bar H B' \;\;\text{(BEKK)},\qquad \bar Q = \frac{1}{T}\sum_t u_t u_t' \;\;\text{(DCC)}$$
  • The intercept matrix \(C\) (that is \(N(N+1)/2\) parameters) drops out of the numerical optimisation
  • The optimiser only searches over the few dynamics parameters - dramatically more stable in high dimensions
  • The model's implied unconditional covariance then matches the data by construction: \(E[H_t]=\bar H\)
The paper imposes covariance or correlation targeting "when possible" precisely to keep 125 models estimable on 10 assets, re-estimated 113 times each over the rolling forecast exercise.

3 · The method

The object: a whole matrix, every day

Returns \(r_t = \mu + \varepsilon_t\) with information set \(\Im_{t-1}\). The target is the conditional variance matrix

$$H_t = E[\varepsilon_t \varepsilon_t' \,|\, \Im_{t-1}]$$
  • \(H_t\) has \(N(N+1)/2\) distinct elements - 55 for the paper's \(N=10\), each moving every day
  • \(H_t\) must be positive definite at every \(t\) - the binding design constraint for all MGARCH models
  • Three families in the paper: direct generalisations of GARCH (BEKK, RiskMetrics), linear combinations of univariate GARCH (orthogonal/factor models), and nonlinear combinations (CCC, DCC and relatives)

BEKK and RiskMetrics (the direct route)

The BEKK(1,1,1) model of Engle and Kroner (1995) - equation (1) of the paper:

$$H_t = CC' + A\,\varepsilon_{t-1}\varepsilon_{t-1}'\,A' + B\,H_{t-1}\,B'$$
  • Quadratic forms keep \(H_t\) positive definite by construction
  • Full BEKK: \(A, B\) unrestricted - "not feasible for large cross-sectional dimensions" (the paper drops it)
  • Diagonal BEKK: \(A, B\) diagonal; scalar BEKK: \(A = aI_N\), \(B = bI_N\) - two parameters after targeting
  • RiskMetrics (J.P. Morgan, 1996): integrated scalar case, smoothing fixed at \(b = 0.96\), \(a = 1-b\), \(C=0\): no estimation at all, "widely used by practitioners"

The orthogonal family (equations (2)-(4)) instead rotates returns into uncorrelated principal components, fits a univariate GARCH to each, and rotates back.

DCC: model variances and correlations separately

The dynamic conditional correlation model of Engle (2002) - equations (5)-(7) of the paper:

$$H_t = D_t^{1/2}\,R_t\,D_t^{1/2},\qquad D_t=\operatorname{diag}(\sigma^2_{1,t},\dots,\sigma^2_{N,t})$$ $$Q_t = (1-a-b)\,\bar Q + a\,u_{t-1}u_{t-1}' + b\,Q_{t-1},\qquad R_t = (Q_t \odot I_N)^{-1/2}\, Q_t\, (Q_t \odot I_N)^{-1/2}$$
  • \(u_{i,t} = \varepsilon_{i,t}/\sigma_{i,t}\) are the devolatilised (standardised) innovations
  • \(a\): how hard yesterday's shock pair moves today's correlation (news); \(b\): how long it lasts (memory); \(a+b<1\) keeps correlations mean-reverting to the target \(\bar Q\)
  • CCC (Bollerslev, 1990) is the special case \(a=b=0\): correlations constant at the sample value
  • Relatives: DCC of Tse and Tsui (2002), asymmetric DCC (Cappiello et al., 2006), equicorrelation DECO (Engle and Kelly)

Two-step estimation: why DCC scales

  1. Step 1 - variances: fit \(N\) separate univariate GARCH models (any flavour: GARCH, GJR, EGARCH, FIGARCH ...), one per asset; form \(u_{i,t} = \varepsilon_{i,t}/\hat\sigma_{i,t}\)
  2. Step 2 - correlations: set \(\bar Q = \tfrac1T\sum_t u_t u_t'\) (correlation targeting), then maximise the correlation part of the likelihood over just \((a, b)\):
    $$\log L_2 = -\tfrac12 \sum_t \big( \log|R_t| + u_t'R_t^{-1}u_t - u_t'u_t \big)$$
  • "Although inefficient, this procedure is consistent and it dramatically reduces the computational burden" - the optimiser never sees more than a handful of parameters at once
  • This is exactly what the interactive laboratory on the simulation slides does, live, on the paper's data

Scoring forecasts: loss functions (equations 10-13)

Compare the forecast \(H_t\) with an ex post proxy \(\hat\Sigma_t\) for the unobservable truth:

LossFormulaCharacter
Euclidean \(L_E\)\(\operatorname{vech}(\hat\Sigma_t-H_t)'\operatorname{vech}(\hat\Sigma_t-H_t)\)symmetric, unique elements once
Frobenius \(L_F\)\(\operatorname{Tr}[(\hat\Sigma_t-H_t)'(\hat\Sigma_t-H_t)]\)symmetric, covariances counted twice
Stein \(L_S\)\(\operatorname{Tr}[H_t^{-1}\hat\Sigma_t] - \log|H_t^{-1}\hat\Sigma_t| - N\)asymmetric: punishes under-prediction
\(L_3\) (\(b=3\))weighted matrix power lossasymmetric: punishes over-prediction
  • All four are robust in the Laurent-Rombouts-Violante (2009)/Patton sense: replacing the truth by an unbiased proxy does not distort the ranking of models
  • The paper's proxy is 5-minute realised covariance; intraday data for 1988-99 are proprietary, so our labs use the outer product \(\varepsilon_t\varepsilon_t'\) - noisier, but unbiased, so rankings survive

The model confidence set, in one slide

Hansen, Lunde and Nason's MCS answers: which subset of models contains the best one, at confidence level \(1-\alpha\), given how noisy the loss differences are?

  1. Start with all models; compute loss differentials \(d_{ij,t} = L_{i,t} - L_{j,t}\)
  2. Test \(H_0: E(d_{ij,t}) = 0\) for all pairs, using a block bootstrap for the distribution of the deviation statistic
  3. If rejected, eliminate the model with the worst standardised performance; repeat until no rejection
  4. Survivors form the \((1-\alpha)\) model confidence set; the paper uses \(\alpha = 0.25\), block length 2, 10,000 bootstraps
Why not just pick the lowest average loss? With noisy proxies and 125 similar models the sample winner changes with the sample. The MCS keeps every model whose inferiority cannot be established - an honest confidence statement about model selection. The SPA test (Hansen, 2005) instead asks whether one pre-chosen benchmark is beaten by any rival.

4 · Interactive laboratory

🎛️ Sim 1: a DCC(1,1) laboratory on the paper's data

KO, PepsiCo and P&G, the last 2,000 days of the paper's estimation sample (April 1991 to December 1998). Univariate GARCH(1,1) filters are pre-estimated by maximum likelihood; you drive the correlation recursion.

Reading the results. With a = b = 0 you get CCC: three flat lines at the sample correlations (0.44, 0.43, 0.33). Raise a and correlations spike whenever two stocks are shocked together; raise b and those spikes decay slowly. Press fit a, b by MLE: the likelihood picks a ≈ 0.016, b ≈ 0.933 (persistence 0.949) - small news impact, long memory, exactly the shape Engle (2002) found. A researcher reads the peaks (1997-98, the Asian and LTCM crises) as evidence that correlations rise in stressed markets, which is why constant-correlation hedges fail when they are needed most.

🎛️ Sim 2: CCC vs DCC vs EWMA, a covariance forecast race

Each model produces a 1-step covariance matrix forecast every day over the last 800 days (June 1995 to December 1998); forecasts are scored against the outer-product proxy \(\varepsilon_t\varepsilon_t'\). The chart shows each model's cumulative average loss advantage over CCC (above zero = beating CCC).

Reading the results. This is the paper's question in miniature. Under the Frobenius loss DCC edges out CCC (374.6 vs 374.9 average) and both beat EWMA (379.1); the gap opens almost entirely during the turbulent 1997-98 stretch and is nearly flat over calm 1995-96 - the paper's finding that "over calm periods ... constant conditional correlation cannot be rejected" while DCC-type models win in instability. Switch to QLIKE: the ordering survives here (DCC 5.247, CCC 5.280, EWMA 5.352) but the gaps change shape, because QLIKE punishes under-predicted variance far more than over-predicted. The loss function choice is not innocent - it is why the paper reports four of them.

🎛️ Sim 3: the curse of dimensionality

Parameters each model asks the optimiser to find (orders 1, variance/correlation targeting imposed as in the paper), as the number of assets \(N\) grows. Log scale.

Reading the results. At the paper's \(N=10\): a full VEC would need 6,105 parameters and a full BEKK 200 - hopeless against 55 distinct data moments per day - while diagonal BEKK needs 20, DCC-GARCH(1,1) 32 (three per stock plus a and b), scalar BEKK 2 and RiskMetrics none. This is exactly why the paper restricts attention to feasible families (Table I) and why "the first generation of models ... are tractable only for a small number of series, typically lower than four". DCC's trick: the 45 correlation targets come free from sample moments, so the optimiser's problem barely grows with N.

🎛️ Sim 4: model confidence set intuition

A teaching-grade MCS on the three racers from Sim 2: block-bootstrap the daily loss differentials, eliminate the worst model while its inferiority is significant, stop when the survivors are statistically indistinguishable. (The paper uses Hansen, Lunde and Nason's full procedure on 125 models, \(\alpha=0.25\), 10,000 bootstraps.)

Reading the results. Under the Frobenius loss at the paper's \(\alpha=0.25\), EWMA is eliminated first (bootstrap p ≈ 0.04) but the procedure then stops (p ≈ 0.6): the MCS is {CCC, DCC} - their 0.07% loss gap could easily be luck, so the honest conclusion is "we cannot separate them on these data". Switch to QLIKE and the elimination bites harder: both EWMA and CCC go, leaving {DCC} alone - exactly the paper's pattern of asymmetric losses producing systematically smaller sets (Stein loss: 10 models vs Euclidean: 39). Slide \(\alpha\) down and sets grow: a smaller \(\alpha\) demands stronger evidence before discarding a model.

6 · Robustness checks

🎛️ Sim 5: is the ranking stable?

The paper's three robustness axes, rebuilt on its own data: forecast horizon (1, 5, 20 days, non-overlapping), evaluation subsample (calm 1995-97 vs turbulent 1997-98), and the loss function (Sims 2 and 4 toggle Frobenius/QLIKE). Bars show average Frobenius loss relative to CCC = 100 (lower = better).

Reading the results. At H = 1 the three models are within about 1% of each other. Stretch the horizon and the ranking spreads out exactly as in the paper's Table IV: at H = 20 EWMA's flat forecast is about 24% worse than CCC (13,884 vs 12,551 raw loss) because "the k-step-ahead forecast for the RiskMetrics type models ... coincides with the one-step-ahead forecast independently from the forecast horizon", while mean-reverting GARCH forecasts glide back towards the long-run covariance. Split the sample and the turbulent half carries losses about 13 times the calm half (695 vs 54) - short unstable stretches dominate full-sample rankings, the paper's central warning.

The paper re-estimates every 22 days on a rolling window of 2,740 observations. How much does the window matter? DCC on KO, PEP, PG (from code/lecture09.py):

7 · Results and discussion

What the labs told us, and what the paper adds

  • Our three-model race on the paper's own data reproduces its qualitative findings in miniature: correlation dynamics matter most in turbulent markets; in calm stretches CCC is statistically indistinguishable from DCC
  • Loss functions change rankings - symmetric losses keep large model sets alive, asymmetric ones discriminate; the paper finds orthogonal models under-predict (favoured by \(L_3\)) and DCC-type models over-predict (favoured by Stein)
  • Horizon matters: integrated/RiskMetrics forecasts are flat and fail at 5-20 days; mean reversion wins there
  • The paper's bottom line (SPA, Table V): Engle's DCC with EGARCH leverage in the variances is never significantly beaten across 124 rivals, three horizons and four regimes - a defensible default for applied work
The MCS habit: when models are close and proxies noisy, report the set of statistically superior models, not just the sample winner. At \(\alpha=0.25\), full sample, that set still held 39 models.

Using this in your dissertation

  • Any multivariate risk question - dynamic hedge ratios, minimum-variance portfolios, VaR of a book, contagion - needs an \(H_t\) forecast: start from DCC-GARCH(1,1), add leverage (GJR/EGARCH) if your assets are equities
  • Estimation: Stata mgarch dcc, R rmgarch::dccfit, or the transparent two-step scipy code from this deck; always impose variance/correlation targeting for N above 3 or 4
  • Evaluation: hold out a forecast sample, choose a robust loss (Frobenius or QLIKE), use squared returns or realised covariance as the proxy, and run the MCS (R package MCS) rather than crowning the lowest average loss
  • Robustness section: repeat over horizons, subsamples and at least two loss functions - your examiner is looking for exactly the axes this paper made standard
  • Scope your ambition: N = 3 to 10 assets is the feasible zone for coursework; beyond that you are in factor and shrinkage territory (Engle's 2009 book is the map)
