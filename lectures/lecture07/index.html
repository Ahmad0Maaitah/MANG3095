1 · The paper

The question

Financial crises recur, and during crises volatility spills over across markets. Diebold and Yilmaz want a measure that can quantify and monitor those spillovers: an "early warning system" for emerging crises and a tracker for ongoing ones.

The gaps this paper fills. Their 2009 Economic Journal index had two methodological limits: it relied on Cholesky-factor identification, so results could depend on the ordering of the variables, and it measured only total spillovers. Substantively, it covered only equity markets across countries.
  • Fix 1: a generalised VAR variance decomposition (Koop, Pesaran and Potter 1996; Pesaran and Shin 1998), invariant to ordering
  • Fix 2: directional spillovers: from each market, to each market, and in net terms
  • New ground: spillovers across asset classes within the US, not just across countries

The data

  • Four US asset classes: stocks (S&P 500), bonds (10-year Treasury yield), FX (NYBOT US dollar index futures), commodities (Dow Jones/UBS commodity index)
  • Daily data, 25 January 1999 to 29 January 2010: 2771 observations, spanning the global financial crisis
  • The object modelled is daily range-based volatility (Parkinson 1980), from daily high and low prices:
$$\tilde\sigma^2_{it}=0.361\,\big[\ln(P^{\max}_{it})-\ln(P^{\min}_{it})\big]^2$$

annualised as \(\hat\sigma_{it}=100\sqrt{365\cdot\tilde\sigma^2_{it}}\) (their Fig. 1; log volatility summary in their Table 1).

The framework itself is generic: it applies to "returns or return volatilities (or, for that matter, any return characteristic of interest)". We exploit that this week: we teach the machinery on returns, and say so explicitly.

Why it matters for you. The connectedness toolkit is one of the most used empirical designs in finance dissertations: pick a set of markets, compute the table, roll it through time, and tell the story of who transmits and who receives. This week you build every piece of it.

2 · Preparing the data and model

Our data: FX returns (and how they differ from the paper's volatilities)

  • We use fx_returns: daily % log changes of the EUR, GBP and JPY dollar exchange rates, 15 December 1998 to 3 July 2018, T = 7141 (positive = the currency weakens against the USD)
  • Sample statistics: means ≈ 0, daily sd 0.46 (EUR), 0.42 (GBP), 0.47 (JPY) percent
  • Largest single move: GBP +6.35% on 24 June 2016, the morning after the Brexit referendum
Be explicit about the object you model. Diebold and Yilmaz measure spillovers in range-based volatilities (Parkinson estimates from daily highs and lows). We teach the machinery on returns: the mathematics is identical, the interpretation changes to return spillovers. In your own work, state clearly whether your table measures return or volatility connectedness, and construct the volatility series first if it is the latter (range-based estimates, realised volatility, or GARCH conditional volatilities all appear in this literature).

3 · The method

Step 1: a VAR and its moving-average form

Start from a covariance-stationary N-variable VAR(p):

$$x_t=\sum_{i=1}^{p}\Phi_i\,x_{t-i}+\varepsilon_t,\qquad \varepsilon_t\sim(0,\Sigma)$$

Each equation is estimated by OLS on the lags of all variables. Inverting gives the moving-average representation \(x_t=\sum_{i=0}^{\infty}A_i\,\varepsilon_{t-i}\), whose coefficient matrices follow the recursion

$$A_i=\Phi_1A_{i-1}+\Phi_2A_{i-2}+\dots+\Phi_pA_{i-p},\qquad A_0=I_N,\ A_i=0\ \text{for } i<0$$

The \(A_i\) matrices are the key to the system's dynamics: variance decompositions parse the H-step-ahead forecast error variance of each variable into parts attributable to each shock.

Step 2: the generalised FEVD (the paper's eq. 1)

Orthogonalising shocks by Cholesky makes the answer depend on variable ordering. The KPPS generalised decomposition instead allows correlated shocks and accounts for them via the observed error distribution:

$$\theta^g_{ij}(H)=\frac{\sigma_{jj}^{-1}\sum_{h=0}^{H-1}\big(e_i' A_h \Sigma\, e_j\big)^2} {\sum_{h=0}^{H-1}\big(e_i' A_h \Sigma A_h' e_i\big)}$$
  • \(\theta^g_{ij}(H)\): the share of variable \(i\)'s H-step forecast error variance due to shocks in variable \(j\)
  • \(\Sigma\) is the residual covariance matrix, \(\sigma_{jj}\) its jth diagonal entry, \(e_i\) the ith selection vector
  • Because shocks are not orthogonalised, rows do not sum to one, so normalise each row (eq. 2):
$$\tilde\theta^g_{ij}(H)=\frac{\theta^g_{ij}(H)}{\sum_{j=1}^{N}\theta^g_{ij}(H)} \quad\Rightarrow\quad \sum_{j=1}^{N}\tilde\theta^g_{ij}(H)=1,\qquad \sum_{i,j=1}^{N}\tilde\theta^g_{ij}(H)=N$$

Step 3: from the FEVD to spillover measures

Total spillover index (eq. 3)
$$S^g(H)=\frac{\sum_{i\ne j}\tilde\theta^g_{ij}(H)}{N}\cdot 100$$
The average share of forecast error variance coming from other markets: one number for system-wide connectedness.
Directional and net (eqs. 4-6)
$$\text{FROM}_i=\textstyle\sum_{j\ne i}\tilde\theta^g_{ij}\cdot 100,\quad \text{TO}_i=\textstyle\sum_{j\ne i}\tilde\theta^g_{ji}\cdot 100$$
$$\text{NET}_i=\text{TO}_i-\text{FROM}_i$$
Positive NET: a net transmitter. Negative: a net receiver.

Notation note: the paper's eqs. (4)-(5) divide the directional sums by N so they aggregate to the total index; its Table 2 reports the plain row and column sums shown here (each row of the table sums to 100). Both conventions appear in the literature. Eq. (7) also defines net pairwise spillovers, \(\big(\tilde\theta^g_{ji}-\tilde\theta^g_{ij}\big)\cdot 100/N\), for market pairs.

How to read a connectedness table

shock to EURshock to GBPshock to JPYFROM others
FEV of EURown shareGBP → EURJPY → EURrow sum, off-diagonal
FEV of GBPEUR → GBPown shareJPY → GBP...
FEV of JPYEUR → JPYGBP → JPYown share...
TO otherscolumn sum, off-diag.......Total index = mean FROM
  • Cell (i, j): the % of market i's H-step forecast error variance explained by shocks originating in market j
  • Rows sum to 100 by the normalisation in eq. (2): each market's forecast error variance is fully parsed into own + cross shares
  • Columns need not sum to 100: a market can explain a lot (or a little) of everyone else's variance
  • The table is an approximate "input-output" decomposition of system-wide risk

4 · Interactive lab

🎛️ Sim 1: the connectedness table, live

VAR(p) estimated equation by equation on the embedded data (the most recent 2000 observations of fx_returns, January 2013 to July 2018), then the generalised FEVD at horizon H. Move the sliders: the whole table re-estimates.

Reading the results. Each cell (i, j) is the share of row market i's H-step forecast error variance due to shocks from column market j; the diagonal is the own share, and every row sums to 100 by construction (eq. 2). Here EUR and GBP are tightly linked (their mutual cells are around 20), while JPY is the most self-contained row: its variance is about 86% home-grown. At p = 2, H = 10 the total index is 22.74% on this 2013-2018 subsample (23.68% on the full 7141-day sample: see the Python cell in section 5). Nudge p from 1 to 4 or H from 2 to 20 and watch how little the table moves: for a researcher this is the first sign that measured connectedness is a property of the data, not of the tuning choices.

🎛️ Sim 2: the rolling total spillover index

Total connectedness re-estimated on rolling windows (VAR(2), H = 10, every 5th day) across 2013-2018. Shorten the window and the index becomes more nervous; lengthen it and episodes smooth out. The dashed line is the full-subsample average.
Reading the results. The full-sample table hides the dynamics: rolling estimation reveals them, exactly as the paper's Fig. 2 does for US asset classes (10-20% in calm times, above 30% in the crisis). On our FX panel with w = 200 the index averages about 29% and swings between roughly 14% and 45%: it peaks in late 2013 (Abenomics yen swings and the Fed taper tantrum), stays high through the 2014-15 dollar surge and ECB QE, and climbs again into early 2017 as windows absorb the Brexit vote (marked) and its aftermath. A researcher reads spikes as periods when a common shock dominates all markets, so forecast errors become mutually explainable, and reports the window-length sensitivity you can generate here as a robustness figure.

🎛️ Sim 3: net directional spillovers - transmitters and receivers

NET = TO - FROM per currency, rolling VAR(2), H = 10, w = 200. Drag the scrubber or press play.
Reading the results. This is the paper's title in one picture: "better to give than to receive". A positive bar marks a net transmitter of shocks, a negative bar a net receiver. Over 2013-2018 EUR is the habitual transmitter (mean net about +3.8), JPY the habitual receiver (about -3.2, consistent with its passive safe-haven role), while GBP swaps roles violently: it swings from -9 to +11, turning into the system's dominant transmitter around sterling-specific shocks such as the Brexit vote. The paper finds the same reversal for US stocks, whose net spillover stayed positive through 2008-2009 and peaked near +7% after Lehman. In a dissertation, the net plot is where the economic story lives: name the transmitter, date the switch, explain the event.

🎛️ Sim 4: Cholesky vs generalised - why ordering matters

Same data, same VAR(2), same H = 10. Toggle between the generalised FEVD and the Cholesky FEVD, then cycle through the 6 possible orderings of three variables and watch the Cholesky table shift while the generalised one never moves.
Reading the results. This is the paper's core methodological motivation. Under Cholesky identification the first-ordered variable absorbs contemporaneous correlation, so every cell, and the total index (13.23% to 14.98% across the six orderings here), depends on an arbitrary ordering choice; with N = 4 markets the paper would face 24 different tables. The generalised decomposition is identical under every ordering (22.74%). Note the level difference is by construction, not a bug: generalised shocks stay correlated, so common news is credited to both markets at once, inflating cross shares relative to Cholesky. Compare indices only within one identification scheme, and if you must use Cholesky, show your results are robust across orderings.

import numpy as np
# teaching-grade GFEVD in numpy - full listing in code/lecture07.py
def var_ols(X, p):                      # equation-by-equation OLS
    T, N = X.shape
    Z = np.hstack([np.ones((T-p, 1))] + [X[p-i-1:T-i-1] for i in range(p)])
    B = np.linalg.lstsq(Z, X[p:], rcond=None)[0]
    E = X[p:] - Z @ B
    Sigma = E.T @ E / (T - p - N*p - 1)
    return np.stack([B[1+i*N:1+(i+1)*N].T for i in range(p)]), Sigma

def gfevd(Phi, Sigma, H):               # Pesaran-Shin, row-normalised
    N = Sigma.shape[0]; A = [np.eye(N)]
    for h in range(1, H):
        A.append(sum(Phi[i] @ A[h-1-i] for i in range(min(h, len(Phi)))))
    num = sum((Ah @ Sigma)**2 / np.diag(Sigma) for Ah in A)
    den = sum(np.diag(Ah @ Sigma @ Ah.T) for Ah in A)
    th = num / den[:, None]
    return th / th.sum(axis=1, keepdims=True)

tsline tci
' EViews: rolling VAR variance decompositions require a program loop:
'   for !s = 1 to 6941 step 5
'     smpl @first+!s @first+!s+199
'     fxvar.ls 1 2 eur gbp jpy
'     ... store the decomposition ...
'   next
' The dialogs do not automate this; the R route below is far quicker.
library(ConnectednessApproach)
dca <- ConnectednessApproach(X, nlag = 2, nfore = 10,
                             window.size = 200, model = "VAR")
PlotTCI(dca)        # rolling total connectedness (paper's Fig. 2)
PlotNET(dca)        # net directional spillovers  (paper's Fig. 5)
PlotNPDC(dca)       # net pairwise spillovers     (paper's Fig. 6)
def rolling_tci(X, p, H, w, step=5):
    out, ends = [], []
    for s in range(0, len(X) - w + 1, step):
        Phi, Sigma = var_ols(X[s:s+w], p)
        th = gfevd(Phi, Sigma, H)
        out.append((th.sum() - np.trace(th)) / X.shape[1] * 100)
        ends.append(s + w - 1)
    return np.array(ends), np.array(out)

ends, tci = rolling_tci(X, 2, 10, 200)
print(tci.mean(), tci.min(), tci.max())

Using this in your dissertation

  1. Pick a system, 3 to 10 markets: currencies, sector indices, crypto assets, commodity futures, country ETFs
  2. Choose and defend the object: returns (return spillovers) or a volatility proxy (range-based as in the paper, realised, or GARCH conditional volatility): state which and why
  3. Report the full-sample table (own shares, FROM, TO, NET, total index), then the rolling index and net plots, dating every spike against real events
  4. Robustness: vary p, H, window length, and split the sample: one figure and one table usually suffice
  5. Tooling: R's ConnectednessApproach for the standard pipeline; your own 40 lines of numpy when you need to see (and defend) every step. Next week extends this exact framework to TVP-VAR connectedness (Antonakakis, Chatziantoniou and Gabauer 2020), which replaces rolling windows with time-varying parameters
Common pitfalls. Mixing return and volatility connectedness when comparing papers; comparing generalised index levels against Cholesky ones; windows shorter than the number of VAR parameters times ten; and reading every spike as "contagion": the framework deliberately sidesteps that word, measuring spillovers without claiming a mechanism.
