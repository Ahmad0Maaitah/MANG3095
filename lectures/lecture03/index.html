What excellent research looks like

Novel research question
A well-defined question that addresses an unexplored angle in your chosen domain.
Clear literature gap
A demonstrable omission in existing scholarship that your study will fill.
Identified data sources
Accessible, appropriate datasets that enable robust empirical testing.
Credible identification
Sound methodology addressing endogeneity and establishing causal claims.

Common pitfalls examiners flag

Structural weaknesses
  • Vague or overly broad research questions
  • Misalignment between the RQ and the literature gap
  • Method unsuited to the research design
Execution concerns
  • Data availability not verified in advance
  • No robustness or sensitivity analysis planned
  • Feasibility unrealistic for a 12 to 16 week timeline
The key: ensure tight integration between your RQ, gap, method, identification strategy, data, and contribution.

Quick check: spot the proposal pitfall

An excerpt from a draft proposal reads: "I will study the effect of interest rates on the economy using appropriate econometric methods on available data." Which pitfall is this?
Plagiarism
Vagueness: no variable, market, period, method or dataset is named, so feasibility cannot be judged
Too narrow a scope
Missing references
Every noun is generic: "the economy", "appropriate econometric methods", "available data". A rigorous proposal names the outcome, the sample, the identification and the data source, which is what the blueprint slide requires.

Identifying genuine literature gaps

Live example: ESG rating divergence

Major providers (MSCI, Sustainalytics, Refinitiv) frequently assign dramatically different scores to the same firm. This creates opportunities to examine:

  • Scope differences: which activities each provider includes
  • Measurement approaches: raw metrics versus weighted composites
  • Weighting schemes: how the E, S and G pillars combine
Recent studies document correlations below 0.5 between providers, suggesting substantial room for rigorous new tests of consequences and determinants.

Spotting environmental-social trade-offs

The research opportunity: firms may improve environmental scores whilst sacrificing social performance. Investigating when and why these trade-offs occur remains underexplored.
  • Potential mechanisms: resource reallocation, stakeholder prioritisation, and regulatory pressure focusing on climate over labour standards.
  • Boundary conditions matter: industry context, national regulation stringency, and firm ownership structure likely moderate these effects.

Sustainable finance project A: environmental-social trade-offs

"Do improvements in environmental scores crowd out social performance?"

Quantify whether gains in "E" dimensions correlate with declines in "S" metrics Identify boundary conditions by industry, regulatory regime, and firm characteristics Inform integrated sustainability strategies and challenge "win-win" assumptions
Identification strategy: panel regressions with firm and time fixed effects to control for unobserved heterogeneity; exploit policy or regulatory shocks as quasi-experiments to strengthen causal inference.
Data sources: Refinitiv (Asset4) granular E, S, G pillar scores and sub-metrics; Compustat/CRSP via WRDS for financial fundamentals and stock returns.
Robustness: compare results across MSCI and Sustainalytics to address provider-specific measurement bias.

Sustainable finance project B: ESG disagreement and asset pricing

"Do portfolios formed on cross-provider ESG disagreement earn abnormal returns?"

DeFi project: protocol valuation

Research question: which key performance indicators (total value locked (TVL), trading volume, fee generation, active users) predict DeFi protocol market capitalisation?

1 · Panel fixed effects2 · Granger causality3 · Lag structures
Control for protocol and time-specific factors influencing valuations Establish temporal precedence between KPIs and market value changes Test varying lag lengths to capture delayed market reactions
Primary data sources: on-chain data extracted directly from blockchain explorers · CoinGecko / GeckoTerminal aggregated protocol metrics and pricing · DEX-specific APIs (Uniswap, SushiSwap, Curve liquidity feeds).
Document your KPI construction methodology meticulously. Address survivorship bias by including defunct protocols, and detail all data-cleaning steps for replicability.

Risk modelling project: improving Value-at-Risk estimates

"Can credibility-augmented VaR improve coverage in cryptocurrency markets?"

Methodology
Implement credibility theory adjustments to standard GARCH-VaR models, blending historical and forward-looking volatility estimates for crypto assets. Compare to benchmark GARCH, EWMA, and historical simulation VaR.
Validation tests
  • Unconditional Coverage (UC) test
  • Conditional Coverage (CC) test
  • Diebold-Mariano test for forecast accuracy

Stretch ideas for students

Extreme value theory: model tail distributions explicitly using the Generalised Pareto Distribution for crypto return extremes.
Systemic risk metrics: CoVaR and VCoVaR to measure spillover effects across DeFi protocols or crypto exchanges.
Intraday risk features: construct realised volatility, jump components, and microstructure noise measures from tick data.

The research onion is from Saunders, Lewis and Thornhill's Research Methods for Business Students.

Identification strategies overview

  1. Natural experiments and DiD: exploit exogenous shocks or policy changes to establish causality.
  2. Instrumental variables: use instruments correlated with the endogenous regressor but uncorrelated with the error term.
  3. Granger causality and panel FE: test temporal precedence whilst controlling for unobserved heterogeneity.
  4. Mediation analysis: decompose total effects into direct and indirect pathways (e.g. algorithmic trading volatility mechanisms).
  5. Robustness checks: pre-specify falsification tests and sensitivity analyses to strengthen credibility.

Why your research matters

Practical relevance: high-quality dissertations influence regulatory policy, inform corporate strategy, and advance market efficiency debates.
  • The Bank of England's Financial Stability in Focus series on AI risk channels exemplifies how academic rigour translates into actionable insights for policymakers.
  • Your contribution should explicitly address implications for regulators, investors, or firm managers.

Writing the micro-literature review

The "Recently, However, Therefore" template

Recently, scholars have documented significant divergence in ESG ratings across major providers (Berg et al., 2022; Gibson Brandon et al., 2021).

However, existing studies focus primarily on measurement causes rather than asset pricing consequences of this disagreement.

Therefore, this study examines whether cross-provider ESG disagreement creates exploitable portfolio alphas.

Anchor your review with 2 to 4 seminal papers that directly address your gap. Demonstrate how your RQ extends or challenges current understanding.

A worked annotated proposal

The module provides a shortened example proposal for teaching purposes ("Bouncing back: can introducing yoyos into UK retail workplaces improve employee productivity and job satisfaction?"). Key points before you read it:

  • The format is not fixed: agree variations to the structure and content with your supervisor, depending on the needs of your research.
  • Choice and flexibility: the example uses a mixed-methods design, but you can choose qualitative, quantitative, or mixed methods. Pick a design that fits the aim and is practical in the time available.
  • Word counts are guidance only, provided you stay within the overall limit of 2,000 words.
  • Use Harvard referencing conventions throughout (SBS requirements).

What the example does well (1/2)

  • Aim with an embedded gap: "Recent research suggests that unconventional workplace practices ... can influence employee engagement and motivation (North, 2022). However, little is known about the specific impacts of these practices on job performance and satisfaction in retail settings."
  • Objectives tied to analyses: "Investigate the relationship between yoyo use and employee productivity via X statistical analysis of ..."; "Explore how yoyo use influences job satisfaction via thematic analysis of ..."; "Synthesise findings by ..."
  • A single clear RQ: "What impact does the use of yoyos in the workplace have on employee productivity and job satisfaction in the UK retail industry?"
  • Literature review that builds to the gap: theories (Self-Determination Theory, Social Exchange Theory), case studies of gamification, then the gap on specific play objects, with reasons for the choice.

What the example does well (2/2)

  • Methodology justified by references: pragmatism as the methodology, a mixed-methods design capturing quantitative productivity metrics and qualitative satisfaction themes, each choice supported by prior studies; limitations stated openly.
  • Data collection specifics: surveys and observations across Southampton retail stores over a two-week trial; sample and population defined; strengths and weaknesses of each instrument; ethical considerations in collection, storage and analysis.
  • Practical value: implications for retail employee engagement strategies, applications for similar play-based interventions, contribution to workplace innovation.
  • Summary sentences everywhere: each section ends by saying what was covered and why it helps the study "fit together" coherently.
For an empirical finance dissertation, replace the mixed-methods elements with your statistical model and measures of the key variables; the architecture stays the same.

Writing workshop

Reporting results

  • The results section clearly describes the findings, usually in both diagrams and text.
  • Do not start to interpret the results: interpretation belongs to the discussion section, which comes next.
  • Data are analysed with statistical techniques and presented in tables, graphs or charts.
Elements of a results section, in order:
Introduction to the results (overview) → statement showing where the results can be found → statement presenting the most important findings → statement commenting on the results (summary, re-organisation to show trends and tendencies, conclusion from the results).

The discussion section

The discussion takes a broad view and puts the research in a wider context, moving from the narrow specific focus to a more general view. It must clearly show how the results lead to the conclusions being drawn, including limitations and possible explanations for the results.

Elements of a discussion section, in order (Weissberg and Buker, 1990; Hopkins and Dudley-Evans, 1988; Swales and Feak, 1994):
Reference to the main purpose of the study → generalised review of the most important findings → possible explanations for the findings → comparison with expected results and other studies → limitations that restrict how far the findings can be generalised.

Writing conclusions

The conclusion shows that the main purpose of the piece of writing has been achieved:

  1. Recall the issues raised in the introduction: what was the purpose of the piece of writing?
  2. Draw together the points made in the main body
  3. Come to a clear conclusion

It should clearly signal to the reader that the writing is finished and leave a clear impression that the purpose has been achieved.

Example (5 sentences): 1During the past 10 years, the use of computers in education has increased dramatically ... 2However, there has been very little research reported on the effectiveness of such use. 3The purpose of the present study was therefore to ascertain the effectiveness of using computer-assisted instruction ... 4The findings clearly suggest that the inclusion of web-based materials ... is effective. 5Further research is needed, however, before the use of such materials can be recommended for all students in all subject areas at all levels.

Useful language: "In brief, ..." · "To conclude, ..." · "It is generally accepted that ..." · "Therefore, it can be concluded that ..." · "On this basis, it may be inferred that ..." · "In conclusion, we may say that ..."

🎯 Check yourself: results, discussion and data

1. Income rose from £100,000 to £200,000 and profit from £80,000 to £140,000. The percentage increase in profit was:
100% 75% 60%
(140,000 - 80,000) / 80,000 = 0.75, so 75%. Income doubled (a 100% rise) but expenditure tripled (a 200% rise), so profit grew more slowly than income.
2. "Figure 3 displays the mean percentile scores on the five subsections of the test." In a results section this sentence is:
A statement presenting the most important findings A statement showing where the results can be found An interpretation of the results
It points the reader to the location of the results (the figure). The headline findings come next; interpretation is saved for the discussion.
3. Where do possible explanations for your findings belong?
The results section The discussion section The references
The results section describes; the discussion interprets: purpose, summary of findings, possible explanations, comparison with other studies, limitations, and further research.

Summary

  • Excellent proposals tie together a novel RQ, a clear gap, credible identification, verified data and an explicit contribution.
  • Four ready domains: sustainable finance, DeFi, algorithmic trading and risk modelling, each with worked designs and data sources.
  • Plan for the 12 to 16 week execution timeline and pre-plan robustness checks.
  • The micro-literature review follows "Recently, However, Therefore", anchored by 2 to 4 seminal papers.
  • Results describe, the discussion interprets, and the conclusion recalls, draws together, and concludes.
