An exciting opportunity

A dissertation is a piece of narrative. It should be a coherent academic story, and you should tell it in such a way that the reader will benefit from reading it. Consequently, you should craft your narrative very carefully; to begin, spend time planning and executing your research effectively and efficiently.

Benefits:

  1. Career opportunities and employability
  2. Strong relationships with your supervisor and with businesses
  3. Intensive research training

Reading the results. Part II · Managing your supervisor

Why manage supervisors?

  • Supervision is a very important part of your supervisor's work, but it is one of many tasks they need to commit themselves to.
  • Your supervisors are probably busier than you think they are.
  • They may not always be as aware of your concerns and progress as you are.
Do you know that your enthusiasm and organisation will encourage them to be focused and engaged?

Managing supervision: six habits

To get the most out of supervision you should take control of the process:

  1. Discuss and agree key issues, especially at the start of the project
  2. Be proactive and arrange formal supervisory meetings
  3. Prepare an agenda and send it to your supervisor in advance
  4. Prepare some work before each meeting (e.g. reading list, data, drafts)
  5. Expect to receive feedback and criticism, and use it to improve your work
  6. Deal with problems as they arise, be they technical, resource or supervision problems
Mutual respect, an understanding of the expectations, and shared commitment to the goal of the research will ensure a smooth partnership.

Part III · Academic integrity

How to avoid a world of pain and tears

Delivered by David Cross, UG Academic Integrity Officer, SBS (d.e.cross@soton.ac.uk).

  • The rules have not changed: you must cite and acknowledge all sources of information in your work.
  • You cannot copy large chunks of text and say it is yours.
  • We use the Harvard referencing system: (Author, Date), for example (Cross, 2022). You cite in text, then put the full reference in a list at the end.
  • Definitive style guide for Southampton Business School: Cite Them Right Online (citethemrightonline.com).
Today: plagiarism, collusion, external authorship. For dissertations, add breaching ethical standards and falsification. Get your ethical approval before arranging anything.

Plagiarism and collusion

Plagiarism: taking credit for someone else's work (including their research). Cite everything, but only if you read it (we can tell!). Read the original, or if not, use "cited in".
Collusion: for example, group work turned into an individual report, or sending your mate your essay (you both fail). It includes conduct after you graduate (fail, and your degree is reclassified). It is really, really easy to spot.

External authorship

  • "Contract cheating" and "essay mills" are now illegal.
  • 52% of purchased essays fail (Sutherland-Smith and Dullaghan, 2019).
  • We have a machine learning tool that flags these to us.
  • Essay mills also resort to blackmail: they threaten to report you to us, and to your future employers.
We will find you. Is it worth the stress and the hassle?

Quick check: is this misconduct?

A student pays a proofreading service that "improves the argument and adds two paragraphs" to the dissertation. Is this misconduct?
No: proofreading is allowed, and this is just very thorough proofreading
Yes, external authorship: content created by someone else is misconduct, even if paid for and even if it is only part of the work
It is only a problem if the marker notices
No, provided the student cites the service in the reference list
Proofreading may correct surface errors only. Adding or reshaping content is external authorship under the university's academic integrity rules: exactly what "How to avoid a world of pain and tears" warned about, and contract cheating services are now illegal.

Writing workshop

The Introduction chapter: the three moves

A strong dissertation introduction makes three moves, in order:

Move 1 · Establishing a research territory: show that the topic matters and summarise what is already known.
Move 2 · Establishing a niche: indicate a gap or a limitation in what has been done so far.
Move 3 · Occupying the niche: state how your study fills the gap, and preview the structure.

Adapted with permission from uefap.com (Gillett); introduced in the Workshop 1 materials.

Language of Move 1: establishing a territory

  • The increasing interest in ... has heightened the need for ...
  • Of particular interest and complexity are ...
  • Recently, there has been growing interest in ...
  • The development of ... has led to the hope that ...
  • The ... has become a favourite topic for analysis ...
  • The study of ... has become an important aspect of ...
  • A central issue in ... is ...
  • The ... has been extensively studied in recent years. / Many recent studies have focused on ...

Language of Move 2: establishing a niche

The most common way to indicate a gap is a "negative" subject, which signals immediately that Move 1 has ended:

  • However, little information / attention / work / data / research ...
  • However, few studies / investigations / researchers / attempts ...

If you prefer to avoid negative comment, use a contrastive statement:

  • The research has tended to focus on ..., rather than on ...
  • These studies have emphasised ..., as opposed to ...
  • Although considerable research has been devoted to ..., rather less attention has been paid to ...
  • Most studies have been content to ... / So far, investigations have been confined to ...

Language of Move 3: occupying the niche

Show that you will fill the gap (or answer the question) created in Move 2:

  • The purpose of this paper / investigation is to ...
  • The aim of this paper is to ...
  • This paper reports on the results obtained ...
  • This study was designed to ...
  • In this paper, the results of ... are presented ...
  • This paper argues that ...
  • This paper is structured as follows ... / The remainder of this paper is divided into five sections ...

Worked example: spot the moves

From Gillett's study of a writing web-site used by pre-Masters students (sentences numbered):

1During the past 10 years, the availability of computers in educational institutions has increased dramatically (James, 1999). 2Progress in computer development has been made to the point that powerful, inexpensive computers with large capacities are available in many classrooms and libraries for student use. 3Many students also have purchased and are purchasing computers for their own use at home. 4Most studies seem to agree that the microcomputer will continue to hold an important role in education in the future. 5For example, James (1999) and Smith (2000) suggest large increases in the numbers of computers both in educational institutions and the home in the near future. 6As far as education is concerned, Shaw (2001) identified three main uses of computers: the object of a course, an administrative tool, and a means of providing instruction. 7Fish and Cheam (2002) cite four uses of computers as a means of providing instruction: exercise, tutorial, simulation and problem solving. 8A wide range of computer programmes are now therefore available in all these areas for individual and classroom use. 9However, even though many studies have reported an increased use of computers in education, there has been very little research reported on the effectiveness of such use. 10The purpose of the present study is therefore to ascertain the effectiveness of using computer-assisted instruction as compared to traditional classroom instruction in an EAP writing class.

Sentences 1 to 8: Move 1 (territory) · sentence 9: Move 2 (niche, "very little research") · sentence 10: Move 3 (occupying the niche, "The purpose of the present study is therefore ...").

Which tenses, and why?

TenseExample from the textWhy it is used
Present perfect"the availability of computers ... has increased dramatically"; "there has been very little research"Recent developments and the state of the field up to now (Moves 1 and 2)
Present simple"Most studies seem to agree ..."; "The purpose of the present study is ..."General truths, current consensus, and stating your own purpose (Move 3)
Past simple"Shaw (2001) identified three main uses of computers"What a specific study found at a specific time

The example introduction: how the flow moves

The annotated example dissertation in the workshop pack (from the Dissertation Library on Blackboard) shows the same moves at chapter scale:

  1. Introduction to the topic: the opening paragraphs set the territory.
  2. Creating a gap or niche: "In spite of ... less evidence ..." Note the use of sources in the introduction.
  3. Filling the gap: introducing this specific research.
  4. Identifying another gap, becoming more specific to this research (the "Chinese" context), and giving the rationale for this specific study.
  5. In this instance the flow widens again before narrowing.
Remember: every dissertation is different and so will use different headings and sub-sections. Study the example's table of contents to see one workable structure.
