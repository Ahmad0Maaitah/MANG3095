1 · The paper

Question and contribution

Question. Average US stock returns vary with size and book-to-market (B/M), but also with profitability and investment. Does a model that adds profitability and investment factors to the Fama-French (1993) three-factor model describe average returns better?

Contribution. A five-factor model with market, size (SMB), value (HML), profitability (RMW) and investment (CMA) factors, motivated by the dividend discount model. It outperforms the three-factor model on every metric, and delivers a striking by-product: with RMW and CMA in the model, HML becomes redundant for describing average returns (Sections 6-7 of the paper).

Prior evidence: Novy-Marx (2013) on profitability; Aharoni, Grundy and Zeng (2013) and Titman, Wei and Xie (2004) on investment.

Data used in the paper

  • Universe: all NYSE, AMEX and NASDAQ stocks on both CRSP and Compustat with share codes 10 or 11
  • Period: July 1963 to December 2013, 606 months
  • Test portfolios (left-hand side): 25 value-weight portfolios from 5x5 sorts on Size-B/M, Size-OP and Size-Inv, plus 32 portfolios from 2x4x4 triple sorts
  • Factors (right-hand side): built from 2x3, 2x2 and 2x2x2x2 sorts; breakpoints always use NYSE stocks only
Variable definitions (for the June year-t sort): OP = revenues minus cost of goods sold, SG&A and interest, divided by book equity (fiscal year t-1). Inv = growth of total assets from t-2 to t-1. B is book equity for fiscal t-1; M is market cap at end of December of t-1.

The patterns to explain (paper Table 1)

Average monthly percent excess returns, 25 value-weight 5x5 portfolios:

Value effect. In every Size row average return rises with B/M. For microcaps it climbs from 0.26 (growth) to 1.15%/month (value); for megacaps only from 0.46 to 0.62. The value effect is stronger among small stocks.
Profitability and investment effects. High OP beats low OP in every Size quintile (Panel B). Low investment beats high investment (Panel C): the microcap portfolio in the highest Inv quintile earns just 0.35%/month, the lowest excess return in the matrix, vs 1.01 for low-Inv microcaps.
The troublemakers. In the 2x4x4 sorts (Table 2), small stocks with low OP and high Inv earn -0.09%/month; small stocks with low B/M and low OP earn 0.03. These portfolios, "small stocks that invest a lot despite low profitability", are lethal for every model in the paper.

2 · Preparing the data and model

The factor data we use

  • ff5_monthly: the Ken French data library five factors, July 1963 to August 2022, T = 710 months (the paper stops in December 2013; we get nine extra years)
  • Columns: Date, Mkt-RF, SMB, HML, RMW, CMA, RF
  • ff5_annual: the same factors at annual frequency, 1964-2021 (58 years)
Units. The factors are already simple returns in percent per month (0.50 means 0.50%, not 50%). No further returns construction is needed - the hard work (CRSP prices, delistings, Compustat book equity) is done by the data library.

Replication note: build the factors yourself from CRSP and Compustat with the tidy-finance.org chapter "Replicating Fama and French factors" (R) or the FamaFrench2015FF5 package on GitHub - links in the code files and references.

Excess returns: why we can regress without subtracting RF

Mkt-RF is the value-weight market return minus the one-month US Treasury bill rate: a classic excess return. The T-bill rate is the RF column.
SMB, HML, RMW, CMA are long-short (zero-investment) portfolios: long one set of stocks, short another. Borrowing costs cancel, so they are already excess returns by construction.
  • The left-hand side of the model is also an excess return, \(R_{it}-R_{Ft}\)
  • A zero-investment portfolio should earn zero excess return unless it carries risk (or mispricing): that is why testing whether mean factor returns and regression intercepts are zero makes economic sense

Stationarity: returns vs price levels

  • OLS on time series needs stationary variables. Factor returns hover around a constant mean with no trend: stationary
  • The cumulative index (a "price level" built from the same returns) wanders: a stochastic trend, not stationary
  • Rule for this week and your dissertation: always regress returns on returns, never price levels on price levels (spurious regression, Week 2 of MANG2074 econometrics)
Check it with the ADF test. The augmented Dickey-Fuller test has \(H_0\): unit root (not stationary). On our data: HML returns ADF = -22.3 (p < 0.001, reject: stationary); the HML cumulative index ADF = -1.9 (p = 0.33, cannot reject: not stationary). Run it yourself below.

3 · The method: why these factors?

The dividend discount model says a share's price is the discounted value of expected dividends; with clean surplus accounting (Miller and Modigliani, 1961) total market value satisfies:

$$m_t=\sum_{\tau=1}^{\infty}\frac{E(d_{t+\tau})}{(1+r)^\tau} \qquad\Longrightarrow\qquad \frac{M_t}{B_t}=\frac{\sum_{\tau=1}^{\infty}E(Y_{t+\tau}-\Delta B_{t+\tau})/(1+r)^\tau}{B_t}$$

Fix everything else and this equation makes three predictions about the expected return \(r\):

higher \(B_t/M_t\) ⇒ higher \(r\) (value)
higher expected earnings \(Y\) ⇒ higher \(r\) (profitability)
higher expected growth of book equity \(\Delta B\) - investment - ⇒ lower \(r\)

So profitability and investment are not arbitrary anomaly patches: they are the natural choices implied by valuation theory (paper Section 1).

From three factors to five

The FF (1993) three-factor time-series regression (paper Eq. 4):

$$R_{it}-R_{Ft}=a_i+b_i(R_{Mt}-R_{Ft})+s_i\,SMB_t+h_i\,HML_t+e_{it}$$

Add profitability (RMW: robust minus weak OP) and investment (CMA: conservative minus aggressive Inv) factors (paper Eq. 5):

$$R_{it}-R_{Ft}=a_i+b_i(R_{Mt}-R_{Ft})+s_i\,SMB_t+h_i\,HML_t+r_i\,RMW_t+c_i\,CMA_t+e_{it}$$
The zero-intercept hypothesis. If the five exposures capture all variation in expected returns, the intercept \(a_i\) is zero for every asset \(i\). Interpretation via Huberman and Kandel (1987): the factors plus the riskfree asset span the mean-variance-efficient tangency portfolio.

Factor construction: the 2x3 sorts (paper Table 3)

Independent sorts into 2 Size groups (NYSE median) and 3 B/M, OP or Inv groups (NYSE 30th and 70th percentiles) give six value-weight portfolios per sort, e.g. SH, SN, SL, BH, BN, BL for B/M:

$$HML=\tfrac{1}{2}(SH+BH)-\tfrac{1}{2}(SL+BL),\qquad RMW=\tfrac{1}{2}(SR+BR)-\tfrac{1}{2}(SW+BW)$$
$$CMA=\tfrac{1}{2}(SC+BC)-\tfrac{1}{2}(SA+BA),\qquad SMB=\tfrac{1}{3}(SMB_{B/M}+SMB_{OP}+SMB_{Inv})$$
  • Equal-weighting small and big legs makes HML, RMW, CMA roughly Size-neutral
  • Alternatives tested in the paper: 2x2 sorts (NYSE medians) and 2x2x2x2 joint sorts; correlations between versions of the same factor are 0.80-1.00 and test results barely change

Reading the results. HML is just the average return gap between the green and red buckets. Squeeze the breakpoints towards the middle (30/70, the paper's 2x3 choice) and HML focuses on the extremes, so the premium is larger; widen them to 50/50 (the 2x2 choice) and every stock is used but the premium shrinks - exactly the paper's Table 4, where 2x3 HML averages 0.37%/month vs 0.28 for 2x2. A researcher reads this as: factor definitions change magnitudes, not conclusions, but always disclose the sort you used.

Tool 1: factor spanning regressions

To ask whether a factor is redundant, regress it on the other four (paper Table 6), e.g.

$$HML_t=a+b\,(R_{Mt}-R_{Ft})+s\,SMB_t+r\,RMW_t+c\,CMA_t+e_t$$
  • The intercept \(a\) is the average HML return left unexplained by exposures to the other four factors
  • \(a=0\): adding HML does not improve the tangency portfolio already spanned by the others - it is redundant
  • \(a\neq 0\): the factor adds distinct information about average returns
Slopes can look counterintuitive. In the paper's HML regression the RMW slope is +0.23 and the CMA slope +1.04: controlling for the other factors, value stocks behave like robust-profitability, low-investment stocks, even though unconditionally value firms tend to be less profitable. Multivariate slopes measure marginal effects, not univariate characteristics.

Tool 2: the GRS joint test (Gibbons, Ross and Shanken, 1989)

With \(N\) test portfolios regressed on \(K\) factors over \(T\) months, test \(H_0: \alpha_1=\alpha_2=\dots=\alpha_N=0\) jointly:

$$GRS=\frac{T-N-K}{N}\;\frac{\hat{\boldsymbol\alpha}'\hat\Sigma^{-1}\hat{\boldsymbol\alpha}} {1+\bar{\boldsymbol\mu}'\hat\Omega^{-1}\bar{\boldsymbol\mu}}\;\sim\;F(N,\,T-N-K)$$
  • \(\hat{\boldsymbol\alpha}\) = vector of estimated intercepts; \(\hat\Sigma\) = residual covariance matrix (N x N)
  • \(\bar{\boldsymbol\mu}\), \(\hat\Omega\) = factor sample means and covariance (K x K): the denominator penalises factors that are themselves hard to earn
  • Why joint? With 25 portfolios, one or two |t| > 2 will happen by chance; the GRS statistic accounts for the correlation between the \(\hat\alpha_i\) and asks whether the whole set is too big

Assumes normal, homoscedastic, serially uncorrelated errors - exact F distribution in finite samples. In the browser we use ST.fCdf; in Python, scipy.stats.f.

🎛️ Sim 3: the alpha map - individual t-stats vs the GRS verdict

N simulated test portfolios with true alphas drawn from N(0, dispersion²) and five-factor exposures, built on the real factor returns (fixed seed). Each is regressed on the five factors; stems are the intercept t-statistics, dashed lines are ±1.96. The readout gives the GRS joint verdict.
Reading the results. Set the true dispersion to zero: stems stay mostly inside the bands and GRS does not reject (it is correctly sized). Raise dispersion slowly: the GRS p-value collapses before many individual stems cross ±1.96 - the joint test pools evidence across portfolios, so it can reject when no single alpha looks significant. Also try raising idiosyncratic volatility: alphas are unchanged but the noise drowns them and power evaporates. This is exactly why the paper leans on the GRS statistic (Table 5) rather than counting significant intercepts - and why with 25 portfolios the occasional |t| > 2 proves nothing on its own.

Step 3: the GRS test

* No built-in GRS command. The user-written grstest2 (Stata Journal)
* implements it:
ssc install grstest2
* GRS 1: are RMW and CMA priced by the FF3 factors?
grstest2 RMW CMA, flist(mktrf SMB HML)
* GRS 2: is HML priced by the other four (N = 1)?
grstest2 HML, flist(mktrf SMB RMW CMA)
* With N = 1 the GRS statistic is essentially the squared intercept
* t-statistic from the spanning regression, scaled by (T-K-1)/T
* and the tangency-portfolio penalty.
' No native GRS command: build it with matrix algebra (see the full
' .prg file for the complete listing). Sketch for N=2, K=3:
equation eq_g1.ls rmw c mkt_rf smb hml
equation eq_g2.ls cma c mkt_rf smb hml
vector(2) alpha
alpha(1) = eq_g1.@coefs(1)
alpha(2) = eq_g2.@coefs(1)
' ... stack residuals into matrix E, then:
' sigma = E'E/(T-K-1),  omega = @cov(factors)
' grs = (T-N-K)/N * a'sigma^-1 a / (1 + mu'omega^-1 mu)
scalar p = 1 - @cfdist(grs, N, T - N - K)
grs_test <- function(assets, factors, data) {
  Fm <- as.matrix(data[factors]); Tn <- nrow(Fm); K <- ncol(Fm)
  N <- length(assets); X <- cbind(1, Fm)
  A <- numeric(N); E <- matrix(0, Tn, N)
  for (i in seq_along(assets)) {
    m <- lm.fit(X, data[[assets[i]]])
    A[i] <- m$coefficients[1]; E[, i] <- m$residuals
  }
  Sigma <- crossprod(E) / (Tn - K - 1)
  mu <- colMeans(Fm); Omega <- cov(Fm)
  grs <- (Tn - N - K) / N * drop(t(A) %*% solve(Sigma, A)) /
         (1 + drop(t(mu) %*% solve(Omega, mu)))
  c(GRS = grs, p = 1 - pf(grs, N, Tn - N - K))
}
grs_test(c("RMW", "CMA"), c("Mkt-RF", "SMB", "HML"), df)  # rejects
grs_test("HML", c("Mkt-RF", "SMB", "RMW", "CMA"), df)     # does not
import numpy as np, statsmodels.api as sm
from scipy import stats

def grs_test(assets, factors, data):
    F = data[factors].values
    Tn, K = F.shape; N = len(assets)
    X = sm.add_constant(data[factors])
    A, E = [], []
    for a in assets:
        m = sm.OLS(data[a], X).fit()
        A.append(m.params["const"]); E.append(m.resid.values)
    A = np.array(A); E = np.column_stack(E)
    Sigma = E.T @ E / (Tn - K - 1)
    mu = F.mean(axis=0); Omega = np.cov(F.T, ddof=1)
    grs = (Tn - N - K) / N * (A @ np.linalg.solve(Sigma, A)) \
          / (1 + mu @ np.linalg.solve(np.atleast_2d(Omega), mu))
    return grs, 1 - stats.f.cdf(grs, N, Tn - N - K)

6 · Robustness checks

What should we stress-test?

  • The paper's own robustness dimension is factor construction: 2x3 vs 2x2 vs 2x2x2x2 sorts change premiums a little (e.g. CMA mean 0.33 vs 0.22 vs 0.14) but never the model rankings (Tables 4-6)
  • Fama and French flag the redundancy of HML as possibly sample specific - so our checks focus on the sample itself:
Subsample stability. Split the 710 months at January 1991 (and anywhere else - the sim below has a slider) and re-run the spanning regressions in each half.
Measurement frequency. Re-estimate everything on the annual factors (58 years): different units, more aggregation, fewer observations.

🎛️ Sim 4: redundancy under the split slider

The spanning alpha of the chosen factor, estimated separately before and after the split date (real data, 95% confidence whiskers). The dashed line is the full-sample alpha.
Reading the results. With the split at 1991, HML's alpha is +0.21 (t = 2.15) pre-1991 but -0.31 (t = -2.45) post-1991: the factor was priced in the first half and earned a significantly negative risk-adjusted return in the second. The full-sample "redundant" verdict is really an average of two different regimes. Now switch to RMW or CMA: their alphas stay positive and significant in both halves wherever you put the slider - genuinely robust. This is exactly the check Fama and French invite when they caution the result "may be specific to this sample", and it is why a dissertation should never report a full-sample alpha without a subsample table.

Discussion. Three lessons. (1) The paper's headline replicates cleanly on the extended sample and at annual frequency: HML's alpha is never reliably positive once RMW and CMA are included. (2) RMW and CMA alphas are stable across every cut: the profitability and investment factors carry information HML does not, in both halves and at both frequencies. (3) The HML verdict is regime dependent: "redundant" over the full sample hides a significantly positive pre-1991 alpha and a significantly negative post-1991 one. The annual regressions lose power (58 observations) but tell the same broad story: measurement frequency does not drive the result, the sample period does.

  • Premiums (Sim 1, Step 1): all five factors earn positive premiums over 1963-2022; CMA has the highest t (3.71) and SMB the lowest (2.00) - the same ordering flavour as the paper's Table 4
  • Redundancy (Sim 2, Step 2): HML's spanning alpha is -0.09 (t = -1.07), absorbed almost entirely by a CMA slope of 1.04; RMW and CMA keep alphas 5 standard errors from zero - paper Table 6 and Section 7 confirmed on nine extra years of data
  • Joint testing (Sim 3, Step 3): GRS rejects FF3 against RMW and CMA (21.7, p ≈ 0) but not the four-factor model against HML (1.13, p = 0.29); the alpha-map sim shows why the joint test, not a count of |t| > 2, is the right tool
  • Fragility (Sim 4): the redundancy verdict flips sign across a 1991 split - a caution the paper itself anticipates

Using the five-factor model in your dissertation

As a benchmark. Testing a trading strategy, an anomaly, a fund or an ESG screen? Regress its excess returns on the five factors (data free from the Ken French library); the intercept is the risk-adjusted performance and its t-statistic is your headline. With several portfolios, report the GRS test too.
Practical checklist. (1) Match frequencies and units (% per month on both sides). (2) Regress returns on returns: run an ADF sanity check if you built the series yourself. (3) Report subsample estimates: full-sample alphas can average away regime changes (our HML result). (4) Say which factor version you use (2x3 is the standard download) and cite the construction. (5) Remember the model's known weak spot: small stocks that invest a lot despite low profitability - if your portfolios load there, expect trouble that is the model's fault, not necessarily your strategy's virtue.
Where the literature went next. Momentum is still missing (Carhart, 1997); Hou, Xue and Zhang (2015) propose a q-factor model with similar profitability and investment factors; FF (2018) add momentum reluctantly. Factor-model horse races remain an active dissertation-friendly area.

Summary - what you can now do

  • Derive the value, profitability and investment predictions from the dividend discount model
  • Write down the five-factor regression and explain the zero-intercept hypothesis
  • Explain how SMB, HML, RMW and CMA are built from 2x3 sorts, and why construction choices matter little
  • Run and interpret factor spanning regressions, including the HML redundancy result
  • Compute and interpret the GRS joint test, and explain why it beats counting significant t-stats
  • Stress-test a factor result across subsamples and frequencies, and report the comparison honestly
