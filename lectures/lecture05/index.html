Why methodology matters

Bridging theory and data
Finance research connects economic theory with empirical evidence. Rigorous methodology enables researchers to test hypotheses, validate theories, and inform policy decisions with credible, data-driven insights.
Integrating multiple dimensions
Financial econometrics combines time-series, cross-sectional and panel techniques to analyse asset prices, risk exposures and volatility patterns. This multidimensional approach captures the complexity of financial markets.
Addressing modern challenges. Contemporary research must handle nonlinear dynamics, structural breaks and heteroscedasticity. Machine learning techniques increasingly complement traditional econometric methods for improved forecasting and risk measurement.

Quick check: which data structure?

You observe annual lending volumes for 3,000 US counties from 2006 to 2016 and want to control for fixed differences between counties. Which data structure and why?
Cross-sectional: one number per county is enough
Time series: lending evolves over time, so only the time dimension matters
Panel: entities tracked over time, so entity fixed effects can absorb unobserved county heterogeneity
None: this requires survey data
Counties (entities) times years (periods) is a classic panel. Fixed effects control for unobserved, time-invariant county differences: exactly the design of the Week 10 paper on FinTech lending.

Sampling and data collection

Define your population. Clearly specify the target population and adopt random sampling techniques to minimise selection bias and ensure representativeness.
Choose appropriate sources. Cross-sectional studies often rely on surveys, financial statements or regulatory filings collected at a single point in time. Ensure data quality and consistency.
Leverage databases. High-frequency trading and macro-finance research require large-scale databases such as CRSP, TAQ, Compustat, or central bank datasets.
Ethical considerations. Data privacy, informed consent and ethical treatment of sensitive information are paramount. Always obtain proper permissions and anonymise data when necessary.

Financial econometrics

Financial econometrics provides the analytical toolkit for understanding asset prices, risk dynamics and market behaviour. These techniques form the foundation of empirical finance research.

1 · Ordinary Least Squares (OLS) and Instrumental Variables (IV)
Baseline techniques for estimating linear relationships. OLS provides efficient estimates under classical assumptions, while IV methods address endogeneity when regressors are correlated with the error terms.
2 · ARCH and GARCH models
Capture conditional heteroscedasticity and volatility clustering in asset returns: volatility varies over time and clusters in periods of high and low volatility.
3 · Advanced econometric methods
Techniques for structural breaks, nonlinear relationships and regime changes; machine learning methods increasingly complement traditional approaches for improved forecasting accuracy.

🎛️ Try it: stationary or not? The AR(1) dial

Every paper week starts by checking stationarity. Drag φ towards 1 and watch mean reversion die: at φ = 1 the series is a random walk, shocks never fade, and the ACF refuses to decay.

Left: a simulated AR(1) series yt = φyt-1 + εt (fixed seed). Right: its sample ACF to lag 20 with a ±1.96/√T band.
Reading the results. Below φ = 1 the ACF decays geometrically (φk) and shocks have a finite half-life, shown in the readout. As φ approaches 1 the half-life explodes and the ACF stays near 1 for many lags: the classic unit-root signature, and the reason you difference prices into returns before modelling them. This is exactly what the ADF test in the warm-up cell at the end of this deck formalises: on real FX returns it rejects the unit root emphatically (statistic near -42), while price levels would not reject.

⚡ Try it: volatility clustering (why GARCH exists)

Financial returns are roughly unpredictable in level but highly predictable in risk: calm days follow calm days, wild days follow wild days. GARCH(1,1) captures that with two numbers.

Simulated GARCH(1,1) daily returns (fixed seed) with the ±2σt conditional band overlaid. α is the reaction to yesterday's shock, β the memory of yesterday's variance.
Reading the results. With α + β near zero the band is flat and returns look iid. Raise the persistence towards 1 and volatility begins to cluster: the band breathes slowly, and the readout shows the ACF of squared returns turning strongly positive even though returns themselves stay serially uncorrelated. That pair of facts, uncorrelated returns but correlated squared returns, is the empirical fingerprint that Engle (1982) and Bollerslev (1986) built ARCH and GARCH around, and Weeks 7 to 9 live entirely inside this world.

Event study example: FinTech and banks

Research question
How do FinTech funding announcements affect traditional European banks' stock prices?
Methodology
A market model event study, using both parametric t-tests and non-parametric rank tests to assess significance. The focus is competitive threats signalled by FinTech funding rounds.
Key findings
  • Significant negative abnormal returns around announcement dates
  • Stronger effects for digital lending FinTechs
  • Results support signalling theory: funding conveys competitive threats
  • Banks with less digital transformation show larger negative reactions
Implications
Traditional banks must accelerate digital transformation to remain competitive. The market perceives well-funded FinTechs as credible threats, particularly in lending and payments; innovation investment and strategic partnerships matter.

🎛️ Event study machine: average CAR around an announcement

Simulated market-model abnormal returns for N bank stocks around an announcement at day 0 (fixed seed). The average CAR cumulates the cross-sectional mean abnormal return; the dashed band is a 95% no-effect confidence band. A negative event-day effect (the FinTech-threat case) drops the CAR through the band; post-event drift mimics slow price adjustment.
Reading the results. With the effect at zero the CAR wanders inside the band: announcements carry no information. A one-off event-day effect shifts the CAR once and it stays flat afterwards, which is exactly what an efficient market implies. If the t statistic on the event-window CAR from day 0 to +10 exceeds 1.96 in absolute value, a researcher would report a significant market reaction; sustained post-event drift instead points to delayed incorporation of news. More events N tighten the band: power grows with the square root of the sample size.

Macro-finance methods

Capturing volatility spillovers
BEKK-GARCH models extend univariate GARCH to multivariate settings, capturing how volatility spills over between financial markets and macroeconomic variables. Dynamic conditional correlation models reveal how shocks in one market propagate to others.
Macroeconomic determinants of risk
  • Inflation: erodes real returns and creates uncertainty
  • Exchange rates: affect multinational earnings and competitiveness
  • GDP growth: reflects economic health and corporate profitability
  • Money supply: influences liquidity and interest rates
  • Interest rates: affect discount rates and borrowing costs
Analytical techniques. Combine Vector Autoregression (VAR), Granger causality tests and structural break detection (the ICSS algorithm) to identify economic shocks, regime changes and causal relationships between macro and financial variables. Weeks 7 and 8 build directly on the VAR toolkit.

Common pitfalls and biases

Data snooping and multiple testing
Testing many specifications and reporting only significant results inflates false discovery rates. Use out-of-sample validation, adjust p-values for multiple comparisons (Bonferroni, FDR), and pre-register research designs when possible.
Omitted variable bias and endogeneity
Omitting correlated variables or reverse causality distorts coefficient estimates and produces spurious conclusions. Address through instrumental variables, fixed effects, control variables, or experimental and quasi-experimental designs.
Overfitting in machine learning
Complex models may fit training data perfectly but perform poorly out-of-sample. Employ k-fold cross-validation, hold-out test sets, regularisation (LASSO, Ridge, dropout) and early stopping.
Survivorship and look-ahead bias
Survivorship bias arises when failed firms drop from databases, artificially inflating performance. Look-ahead bias uses information not available at decision time. Construct unbiased samples and align information timing carefully in backtests.

🪦 Try it: the survivorship bias machine

200 simulated funds whose TRUE average skill is exactly zero. Each year, the worst performers are shut down and vanish from the database. Then you compute "average performance" the naive way, using only the funds still alive at the end.

Average cumulative return of end-of-sample survivors vs all funds including the dead ones (fixed seed). The dashed line is the truth: zero skill.
Reading the results. The all-funds average hugs zero, because that is the truth. The survivors-only average drifts upward purely because losing funds keep being deleted: with 10% annual attrition the fake "alpha" in the readout is worth several percent a year, and it grows with both the attrition rate and the noise. Nothing about the surviving funds is skilled; the sample was filtered on the outcome. This is why serious backtests use point-in-time databases (CRSP includes dead firms for exactly this reason), and it is the trap in the quiz on the next slide.

Quick check: spot the bias

A student backtests a trading rule on the current FTSE 100 constituents over 2000 to 2024 and reports remarkable returns. What is the most fundamental problem?
Overfitting: the model has too many parameters
Survivorship bias: today's constituents exclude firms that failed or were relegated during the sample
Endogeneity: returns cause the trading rule
Multiple testing: too many p-values were computed
Conditioning the sample on being in the index today means every firm in the backtest "survived" to 2024, inflating measured performance. The fix is a point-in-time constituent list. The other pitfalls may also apply, but the sample itself is biased before any model is estimated.

Conclusion and takeaways

Robust methodology matters. Rigorous research design links economic theory with empirical data, enabling credible insights that advance knowledge and inform policy. Your methodological choices fundamentally shape your findings and their impact.

Choose wisely
Select data structures and econometric models that align with your research question. Understand the assumptions underlying each method and verify they are satisfied in your application.
Embrace diversity
Employ diverse techniques: traditional econometrics, event studies, machine learning and network analysis, to capture different dimensions of financial phenomena and ensure robust conclusions.
Maintain standards
Uphold ethical standards in data collection and analysis. Document all procedures for reproducibility. Continue learning as methods and markets evolve.

Week 6 · The five-factor asset pricing model

Fama and French (2015), 'A five-factor asset pricing model', Journal of Financial Economics.

Method. Time-series regressions of portfolio returns on five factors (market, size, value, profitability, investment), factor-spanning regressions, and the GRS test of whether intercepts are jointly zero.

Why it matters. The workhorse model of modern empirical asset pricing: any anomaly or fund manager you ever evaluate is judged against these factors. You will run the regressions on the real Ken French data and see why the value factor becomes redundant once profitability and investment enter.

📊 Go to Week 6

Week 7 · Volatility spillovers

Diebold and Yilmaz (2012), 'Better to give than to receive: predictive directional measurement of volatility spillovers', International Journal of Forecasting.

Method. A VAR on volatilities, generalised forecast error variance decompositions (order-invariant), and total, directional and net spillover indices built from the decomposition table.

Why it matters. The standard way to quantify how shocks travel across markets: who transmits, who receives, and how connectedness explodes in crises. The same framework powers hundreds of applications from equities to crypto.

🔗 Go to Week 7

Week 8 · TVP-VAR dynamic connectedness

Antonakakis, Chatziantoniou and Gabauer (2020), 'Refined measures of dynamic connectedness based on time-varying parameter vector autoregressions', Journal of Risk and Financial Management.

Method. A time-varying parameter VAR estimated with forgetting-factor Kalman filtering, so spillover measures update every day instead of relying on rolling windows.

Why it matters. Fixes the rolling-window dilemma (window length is arbitrary, observations are lost, crises are smoothed). You will watch connectedness move through calm and crisis periods on real FX returns.

🌊 Go to Week 8

Week 9 · Multivariate GARCH forecasting

Laurent, Rombouts and Violante (2012), 'On the forecasting accuracy of multivariate GARCH models', Journal of Applied Econometrics.

Method. A horse race of multivariate GARCH covariance forecasts (DCC and relatives), evaluated with robust loss functions and the model confidence set.

Why it matters. Portfolio risk, hedging and VaR all need a covariance forecast; this paper shows how to compare forecasts honestly when the true covariance is unobservable. You will work with the original 10-stock daily dataset used in the paper.

🧮 Go to Week 9

Week 10 · FinTech lenders and small business lending

Gopal and Schnabl (2022), 'The rise of finance companies and FinTech lenders in small business lending', Review of Financial Studies.

Method. A county-level panel with fixed effects and exposure-based identification: counties where banks had a larger pre-crisis market share saw finance companies and FinTech lenders expand more after 2008.

Why it matters. A real published replication package, dissected: how professional empirical work organises raw data, cleaning code and output tables, and how panel identification and robustness are done in practice. This is the template for your own dissertation workflow.
