Research in finance

  • Research in finance is a broad field that involves studying and analysing various financial aspects, including markets, investments, banking, corporate finance, personal finance and financial economics.
  • Always think about policy implications: why do I have to write this dissertation, and for whom?
  • In other words, the dissertation should provide insights, theories and models that can help individuals, businesses and governments make informed financial decisions.

Key characteristics of research in finance

Objectivity: research should be unbiased and impartial, avoiding personal opinions or preconceived notions. For example, if you study the impact of the Russia-Ukraine war on commodity markets, you should avoid any personal opinion or judgement about the war itself.
Systematic approach: a structured and organised process, with clearly defined research questions, hypotheses, data collection methods and analysis techniques.
Empirical evidence: reliance on data obtained from reliable sources, such as financial statements, market data, experiments or financial databases.
Theory development: contributing to theories and models that explain financial phenomena and guide decision-making.
Generalisability: findings should apply beyond the study's specific context, allowing broader implications and insights.

Good vs bad research practice

Differentiating between good and bad research practices in finance is crucial:

Methodological rigour: good research emphasises rigorous methodologies such as quantitative analysis, statistical techniques and econometric models. These provide a systematic and objective approach to analysing financial data and testing hypotheses.
Data quality: high-quality research relies on accurate and reliable data sources: valid, representative and appropriately collected.
Transparency and replicability: clear documentation of the research process, including data sources, methodology and analysis techniques.

Quantitative vs qualitative analysis in finance

Quantitative

Provides a more objective and systematic approach to analysing financial data, allowing statistical inference, hypothesis testing and generalisability. The preferred primary method in finance research.

Qualitative

Valuable in certain fields, but generally not preferred as the primary method in finance. It can still complement quantitative findings, provide context, or explore subjective aspects of financial decision-making.

Where to start?

  1. Identify the broad topic
  2. Determine the scope and focus
  3. Frame the research questions

The next slides walk each step in turn, ending with a real worked example.

Step 1 · Key areas of research in finance (1/2)

Financial Technology (FinTech): an emerging field using technology to improve financial activities: the impact of blockchain, cryptocurrencies, digital payments, robo-advisors and other innovations on the financial industry.
Investment analysis: stocks, bonds and mutual funds, their potential returns and risks; the development and testing of investment strategies.
Financial markets and institutions: dynamics of markets (stock, bond) and institutions (banks, insurers); market efficiency, asset pricing, interest rates, and the impact of regulatory policies.
Behavioural finance: combining psychology and finance to understand how emotions and cognitive errors influence financial decisions. Be careful here: the data will be difficult to collect.

Step 1 · Key areas of research in finance (2/2)

Personal finance: how individuals manage their money: savings, investments, insurance and retirement planning.
Corporate finance: how corporations raise capital, invest it and manage their financial resources: capital structure, dividend policy, corporate governance, mergers and acquisitions, financial risk management.
Sustainable and responsible finance (including green finance): integrating environmental, social and governance (ESG) factors into investment decisions; the impact of sustainable investing and corporate social responsibility on financial performance.
Financial econometrics: applying statistical methods to financial data to test theories and predict future trends; used in risk management, asset pricing and portfolio management.

Step 2 · Determine the scope and develop focus

Establish a working title: move from broad to specific. A real-life experience example:

  1. Areas of interest: "I am particularly interested in the intersection of finance and technology, to find blockchain technology's and cryptocurrencies' impact on traditional financial systems."
  2. Literature review: "There is a growing body of research on cryptocurrencies and blockchain, but a gap in understanding how these technologies are reshaping traditional banking systems; specifically, limited research on how blockchain could enhance the efficiency, transparency and security of banking operations."
  3. Practicality: "There is a wealth of data available from various sources, plus numerous academic and industry reports, so data availability and resources should not be a significant issue."

Quick check: which question is well scoped?

Pick the research question that is appropriately scoped for an undergraduate finance dissertation.
What causes financial crises?
Do FTSE 100 firms exist?
Do UK equity funds with higher ESG scores show different risk-adjusted returns, 2016 to 2024?
Is money important?
The correct option names the market (UK equity funds), the variable (ESG scores), the metric (risk-adjusted returns) and the period (2016 to 2024), so one dataset can answer it. The others are a whole literature, trivially true, or unanswerable as posed.

Step 3 · Frame the research questions

RQ1. How does the implementation of blockchain technology influence the operational efficiency of traditional banking systems?
Analyse blockchain's impact on the efficiency of banking operations: transaction speed, cost-effectiveness, error rates, and automation of processes.
RQ2. How does blockchain technology enhance the transparency and security of transactions in traditional banking systems?
Explore how blockchain features such as immutability, decentralisation and encryption contribute to greater transparency and heightened security.

Your task now!

  1. Try to have 3 ideas
  2. Make a brief initial literature search
  3. Reflect on the ideas in the light of what has already been written
  4. Refine your ideas and develop your research topic(s)
  5. Consult your supervisor
  6. Evaluate and reflect on your research topic
  7. Finalise the topic
  8. Transform it into research questions

As finance students ...

  • Understand the characteristics and methods of good research in the field.
  • Follow rigorous methodologies, rely on empirical evidence, and emphasise quantitative analysis.
  • Critically evaluate research studies: consider the quality of data and methodology, and seek peer-reviewed sources to ensure reliability and validity.
To achieve the above, check your skills and constraints:
  • For example, language and quantitative skills
  • You can only learn a limited amount of skills in six months
  • Symmetry of potential outcomes (the study is informative whichever way the result goes)
  • Consider your career goals
  • Check you can access the data you need

🎯 Check yourself: scoping a topic

1. Which of these is the best-scoped working title for a 6-month UG dissertation?
Cryptocurrencies and the world economy The impact of blockchain technology on the efficiency of European retail banks Is finance good for society?
It narrows the funnel: broad field (finance and technology) → domain (blockchain and banking) → location (Europe) → measurable outcome (efficiency). The others are too broad to answer with data.
2. Which practical check should come before you finalise a behavioural finance topic?
Whether a Nobel prize was won in the area Whether you can actually access the data you need Whether the topic sounds impressive to employers
Behavioural finance data will be difficult to collect: data access is one of the key feasibility checks alongside skills, time (six months) and symmetry of outcomes.

Writing workshop A

Structuring the literature review

Possible ways to structure the review; pick the one that best fits the story you are telling about the reason for your study, and outline it for your reader:

  • Chronologically: but be careful not just to list items; write critically, not just descriptively
  • By theme: useful if several strands can be considered separately before being brought together
  • By sector: e.g. political, practice, methodological, geographical, literary background
  • By development of ideas: useful if there are identifiable stages of idea development
  • Some combination of the above, or another structure you create

Structure (a): introduction, main body, conclusion

PartWhat it should do
IntroductionDefine your topic and provide context · establish your reasons (point of view) for reviewing the literature · explain the organisation (sequence) of the review · state the scope: what is included and what is not. Example: "There are a large number of studies of obesity trends in the general population. However, since the focus of this research is on obesity in children, these will not be reviewed in detail and will only be referred to as appropriate."
Main bodyOrganise the literature according to common themes · provide insight into the relation between your topic and the wider subject area · move from a general, wider view to the specific focus of your research.
ConclusionSummarise the important aspects of the existing literature · evaluate its current state · identify significant flaws or gaps · outline areas for future study · link your research to the existing knowledge.

Adapted from the Royal Literary Fund guide (rlf.org.uk).

Language for synthesising sources

  • Similarly, Nicoladis (2006) found that X ...
  • In the same vein, Smith (1994) in his book XYZ notes ...
  • This view is supported by Jones (2000) who writes that ...
  • Smith argues that her data support O'Brien's (1988) view that ...
  • Al-Masry's (1986) work on X is complemented by Smith's (2009) study of ...
  • Almost every paper that has been written on X includes a section relating to ...
  • Unlike Smith, Jones (2013) argues that ... / In contrast to Smith, Jones (2013) argues that ...
  • A broader perspective has been adopted by Smith (2013) who argues that ...
  • Conversely, Wang (2010) reported no significant difference in mortality between X and Y ...

From the Academic Phrasebank, University of Manchester (phrasebank.manchester.ac.uk).

Writing workshop B

The methodology chapter

  • The method describes the steps you followed in conducting your study and the materials you used in each step.
  • It should show your reader that your research has been carried out appropriately, and therefore that the results can be believed.
  • The elements included, and their order, may differ from department to department; the list on the next slide is typical and provides a good model (Weissberg and Buker, 1990, p. 92).

Elements of a methods section

  • Overview of the research
  • Theoretical justification
  • Population / sample
  • Location
  • Restrictions / limiting conditions
  • Sampling technique
  • Procedures *
  • Materials *
  • Variables
  • Statistical treatment

* always included

Worked example: a short methodology section

Gillett's study compared on-line vs traditional classroom teaching of academic writing (sentences numbered). Condensed:

1The aim of the research was to compare the success of students using on-line teaching compared to students taught in the traditional classroom context. 2This experimental approach adopted a positivist position by collecting primary quantitative data from the students' performance on an objective test taken after the teaching process ended. 4Two groups of international students on a one-year pre-Masters EAP course, each comprising 50 students, were taught academic writing by different methods and compared. 7The programme accepts only students from a narrow English proficiency band (IELTS 5.0 to 5.5). 9Subjects were selected from the 250 students on the programme on the basis of satisfactory performance in the Semester A examination. 12Group A studied writing in the traditional way in a class with a teacher. 14Group B met a teacher for one hour per week and spent one hour per week on the UEfAP web-site. 15The test instrument was a revised version of the University of Hertfordshire English Language Writing Test. 17Both groups took the same written examination at the end of the semester. 21A 3 x 5 analysis of variance was used to test for academic department, method of teaching and language achievement differences.

🎯 Check yourself: name that element

1. "Two groups of international students, each comprising 50 students, were taught academic writing by different methods." Which element?
Statistical treatment Overview / sample Materials
It gives the overview of the design and the sample size; the test instrument would be "materials" and the ANOVA would be "statistical treatment".
2. "Students who had performed below the minimum level on the Semester A examination were excluded." Which element?
Procedure Sampling technique Theoretical justification
Inclusion and exclusion criteria describe how the sample was selected: the sampling technique.
3. "A 3 x 5 analysis of variance was used to test for differences." Which element?
Restrictions Variables Statistical treatment
The statistical treatment element states which statistical technique analyses the data; in your dissertation this is where the econometric model goes.

Summary

  • Good research in finance is objective, systematic, empirical, theory-building and generalisable; quantitative methods are the preferred primary approach.
  • Scope your topic in three steps: broad topic → scope and focus → research questions, narrowing through domain, location and theory to a working title.
  • This week: develop 3 ideas, search the literature, refine, and consult your supervisor.
  • Structure the literature review around a deliberate story (theme, chronology, sector or idea development) and write critically, not descriptively.
  • A methodology chapter covers overview, justification, sample, restrictions, sampling, procedures, materials, variables and statistical treatment.
