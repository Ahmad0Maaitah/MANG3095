Purpose and strategic scope

A well-crafted literature review serves as the foundation for original research, enabling scholars to understand the current state of knowledge on many areas within finance, such as volatility in traditional finance and emerging cryptocurrency markets.

This systematic approach helps researchers identify theoretical frameworks, established methodologies and empirical findings that shape our understanding of market behaviour.

What is a literature review?

Comprehensive survey
A systematic examination of scholarly works, including peer-reviewed articles, books, theses and working papers on a specific topic.
Theoretical foundation
Summarises key theories such as the Efficient Market Hypothesis and GARCH models, explaining their development and applications.
Research connections
Reveals relationships, conflicts and gaps in existing research, showing how studies build upon or challenge each other.
Contextual positioning
Establishes the foundation for your contribution by situating your work within the broader academic conversation.

Define your research question

01 · Start with clarity - develop a focused topic such as "Volatility patterns in cryptocurrency versus stock markets" or "News impact on commodities price fluctuations".
02 · Determine scope - define asset classes (equities, commodities, crypto), geographic markets and temporal boundaries for your investigation.
03 · Maintain flexibility - refine your question iteratively as you encounter new literature and discover emerging themes in the field.

Your question drives every later choice: keywords, databases, journals, and eventually the data and methods you will meet in Week 5 onwards.

Every search strategy is a funnel: keywords and databases decide how much you find, screening and snowballing decide what you actually read. Move the sliders and watch the papers flow through.

Reading the results. Breadth and databases control what you find: the keyword table two slides back is how you widen the top of the funnel, and each extra database adds coverage but also duplicates. Strictness is your title and abstract screen, the first pass of the critical evaluation framework later in this deck; snowballing (chasing reference lists and "cited by" links) catches papers that keyword searches miss. Whatever lands in the final bar is the set you log, one row per paper, in the evaluating-sources template, so aim for a green verdict: enough papers to synthesise themes, few enough to read critically.

Focus on high-quality journals

Top-tier finance journals. Prioritise ABS 3, 4 and 4* rated journals for credibility and methodological rigour. Leading examples include the Journal of Finance, Journal of Financial Economics and Review of Financial Studies, plus strong field journals such as the Journal of Banking and Finance and Energy Economics.
Quality indicators. Consult the Chartered ABS Academic Journal Guide (AJG 2024) or the Financial Times Top 50 journal list to identify respected publications in your field.
Research reliability. High-quality sources enhance the reliability of your literature review and ensure your work builds upon robust empirical foundations.

Critical evaluation framework

Systematic source assessment. Each source requires rigorous evaluation beyond surface-level reading. Document the research question, methodology, data characteristics and conclusions of every paper you review.

  • Verify peer-review status and author credentials
  • Assess journal ranking and impact factor
  • Determine how it relates to existing work: does it confirm, extend, or challenge previous findings?
  • Identify methodological strengths: innovative approaches, robust data, rigorous analysis
  • Note potential limitations: sample size constraints, assumptions, temporal boundaries

Key research themes: volatility and efficiency

Volatility clustering
Large price movements tend to be followed by large movements, and small changes by small changes: fundamental to financial time series behaviour.
  • Seminal models: ARCH (Engle 1982) and GARCH (Bollerslev 1986) capture clustering in traditional equity markets
  • Crypto applications: Bitcoin returns exhibit similar clustering with potentially higher intensity
  • Measurement: compare realised (historical) versus implied (forward-looking) volatility across asset classes
Market efficiency
The Efficient Market Hypothesis posits that asset prices reflect all available information, tested across three forms: weak, semi-strong and strong.
  • Testing approaches: anomaly studies of momentum, mean reversion and event-driven price movements
  • Cryptocurrency question: do crypto prices rapidly incorporate news, or are there systematic delays?
  • Empirical evidence: regulatory changes and technological upgrades show varying speeds of price adjustment across cryptocurrencies

Key research themes: behavioural finance and crypto

Behavioural biases
Humans frequently deviate from rational decision-making, leading to market anomalies. Loss aversion, overconfidence and herding significantly impact asset prices.
Herding effects
Investors follow the crowd during market bubbles. Bitcoin's 2017 surge exemplifies how retail sentiment can drive extreme price movements beyond fundamental values.
Sentiment analysis
Social media buzz correlates with market swings. Twitter, Reddit and news sentiment indices predict short-term crypto volatility more strongly than for traditional assets.
Cryptocurrency market inefficiencies. Crypto markets, newer and less regulated than traditional markets, exhibit greater inefficiencies: price bubbles, liquidity constraints, information asymmetry and cross-exchange arbitrage opportunities. Research demonstrates higher volatility and lower predictability in smaller-cap cryptocurrencies compared to Bitcoin; pump-and-dump schemes and regulatory announcements create exploitable patterns that challenge the EMH.

Structuring your literature review

Thematic organisation
Group papers by key themes: volatility models, market efficiency, behavioural finance, crypto-specific factors. Highlights conceptual connections.
Chronological structure
Trace theoretical evolution from early ARCH models through modern machine learning applications. Shows how understanding developed over time.
Methodological approach
Compare qualitative versus quantitative methods, traditional econometrics versus AI techniques. Useful for methodology-focused research.
Use clear headings and subheadings to guide readers through your narrative. Most finance literature reviews employ thematic organisation with chronological elements within each theme.

Writing excellence: synthesise, don't summarise

Beyond simple summary. Effective literature reviews compare and contrast multiple sources simultaneously rather than describing papers sequentially. Your analysis should identify patterns, highlight debates and offer critical insights.

MoveWhat it sounds like
Group and compare"Several studies find X, whilst others report Y"
Highlight patterns"Most papers employ GARCH models for both stock and crypto volatility"
Offer analysis"This suggests EMH holds in developed stock markets but is weaker in cryptocurrency markets"
Maintain coherencetopic sentences and transitions give the section logical flow

Common pitfalls to avoid

Listing instead of analysing
Avoid one paragraph summarising each paper sequentially. Integrate multiple sources within thematic discussions to demonstrate synthesis and critical thinking.
Lack of focus
Maintain strict adherence to your defined research question. Resist including tangentially related studies that dilute your narrative's coherence and clarity.
Overlooking recent research
Crypto and volatility research evolves rapidly. Include the latest findings, particularly post-2020 studies addressing COVID-19 impacts and DeFi developments.
Ignoring methodological critiques
Acknowledge when studies challenge each other's methods or conclusions. Discussing controversies and limitations demonstrates sophisticated understanding.

Quick check: synthesis

Which of these sentences belongs in a well-synthesised literature review?
"Smith (2021) used GARCH. The data were daily Bitcoin returns. The next paper is Jones (2022)..."
"Whilst GARCH studies of Bitcoin consistently find strong clustering (Smith 2021; Chen 2022), evidence on asymmetry is mixed, with Jones (2022) reporting no leverage effect"
"This review will list all 42 papers found on Google Scholar in the order they were published"
"Volatility is when prices move a lot, which many papers have studied"
Synthesis means comparing and contrasting several sources in the same sentence or paragraph, surfacing agreement, disagreement and patterns, rather than marching through papers one at a time.

Writing an abstract

The abstract is the first section of the report, after the title and before the introduction (sometimes titled "summary"). It provides an overview of the study based on information from the other sections, so the reader can decide whether to read the complete report. Because it contains elements from the whole report, it is usually written last.

Ordering your information (Weissberg and Buker, 1990, p. 186) - the conventional sequence:
  1. Introduction - introduce the study by describing the context / background; explain why the subject is important
  2. Purpose - describe the purpose of the study
  3. Methods - report how the study was undertaken
  4. Results - report the results that were found
  5. Evaluation - briefly evaluate the results
  6. Conclusion - conclude briefly; explain what is important and why

Abstracts from almost all fields of study are written in a very similar way; the types of information and their order are highly conventional. Your report abstract: max 300 words.
