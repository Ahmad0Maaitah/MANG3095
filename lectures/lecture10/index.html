1 · The paper

The question

  • US business investment fell more than 11% from June 2008 to December 2010; bank lending to small businesses dropped by more than 20% between 2008 and 2010
  • The worry: banks had permanently cut credit to small firms (losses, tighter regulation, stress tests), starving them of finance and dragging down the real economy
  • Gopal and Schnabl ask two things:
    1. Was there a permanent decline in bank lending to small businesses after 2008?
    2. Did that decline reduce real economic activity?
Headline answer: yes, bank lending permanently declined; no, activity did not suffer, because finance companies and FinTech lenders stepped in and replaced the missing bank credit almost one for one.

The contribution: a new loan-level dataset

  • Built from UCC filings: public records that secured lenders file under Article 9 of the Uniform Commercial Code to register their claim on a borrower's collateral
  • 11.26 million loans to 3.4 million unique firms, all 50 states plus DC, 2006 to 2016
  • Covers about 95% of secured non-real-estate small business lending and an estimated 73% of total small business lending (about $699bn of a $958bn market in 2016); the widely used CRA data cover only 43%
  • UCC filings record borrower, lender, date and collateral, but not loan size: the paper counts loans (loan counts and volumes correlate at 90 to 97% where both exist)

Figure 1 of the paper: bank lending fell 26% from 2007 to 2010 and never returned to trend; by 2016 nonbank lenders originated close to 60% of new small business loans.

Identification: county exposure to a nationwide shock

The 2008 shock to banks was nationwide, but counties differed in how much they relied on banks beforehand. Counties with a larger 2006 bank share were hit harder:

Numerical example (the paper's own). Banks everywhere cut lending by 50%. County A has a 90% bank share: total lending falls 0.9 × 0.5 = 45%. County B has a 10% bank share: total lending falls 0.1 × 0.5 = 5%. Same shock, different exposure: that cross-county variation identifies the supply effect.
$$\Delta Y_c \;=\; \alpha_s \;+\; \gamma\,\text{BankShare}_{06,c} \;+\; \delta X_c \;+\; \varepsilon_c \qquad \text{(paper's eq. 1)}$$
  • \(\Delta Y_c\): change from 2007 to 2016 in the nonbank market share, nonbank lending, or total lending in county \(c\)
  • \(\alpha_s\): state fixed effects; \(X_c\): 2005 conditions (unemployment, participation, wage, log population) and 2002 to 2006 pretrends
  • Standard errors clustered at the county level; the identifying assumption is that the 2006 bank share affects post-crisis nonbank lending only through the bank shock

Could it just be demand? Four defences

  • Industry and collateral fixed effects (Table 5): within two-digit SIC industry the coefficient is 0.111*** (double-clustered by county and industry); within collateral type 0.201***. The story survives comparing similar firms pledging similar collateral
  • Within-firm estimator (Table 6): firms that borrowed from both a bank and a nonbank before 2008 (149,175 firms, two observations each) were 3.4 pp more likely to get their next loan from the nonbank, with firm fixed effects absorbing firm-level demand
  • Three natural experiments (Table 7): FAS 166/167 off-balance-sheet consolidation (0.252***), stress-test exposure (0.209***), Dodd-Frank capital increases (0.026***): all shift lending to nonbanks, none moves total lending
  • Bank-owned vs independent finance companies (Table 8): bank-owned finance companies, which face bank regulation, cut back (-0.033***) while independents expanded (+0.043**): regulation, not demand

2 · Preparing panel data

Entity and time identifiers

  • A panel needs two identifiers: an entity id (here: county) and a time id (here: year). Every dataset in the pipeline is keyed on the pair (county, year)
  • US counties have FIPS codes: a 2-digit state code plus a 3-digit county code. The do-files build them exactly as you would:
    gen fips = fipstate*1000 + fipscty    // cbp_creation.do
    gen county = statefips + countyfips   // laus_creation.do (string concat)
    gen state = substr(county,1,2)       // figures_and_tables.do
  • In Stata, xtset county year declares the panel (the panel twin of tsset); in Python/R you simply keep both columns and group by them
  • Composite ids are made the same way everywhere: egen bk_cnty_yr = group(respid agency year state county) builds a bank-county-year id in cra_data_creation.do

Merging six sources on the county id

The whole paper hangs on m:1 merges: many county-year (or county-industry) rows matched to one row of county-level data. From figures_and_tables.do, verbatim:

use $data\final\county_lending_changes_baseline.dta", clear
merge m:1 county using $data\final\pre_crisis_bank_share_county_baseline.dta", keep(3) nogen
merge m:1 county using $data\final\county_pre_crisis_controls.dta", keep(3) nogen
  • keep(3) keeps only matched rows (an inner join): counties missing a bank share or controls drop out, which is why Table 4 has 3,021 counties rather than 3,092
  • The controls file itself is the product of merging CBP (wages, establishments), LAUS (unemployment, participation), SUSB (expansion rate), FDIC deposits and Census population, all keyed on county
  • In pandas: df.merge(county_frame, on="county", how="inner", validate="m:1"); the validate argument is the honest equivalent of Stata's m:1 declaration

Balanced vs unbalanced panels

Balanced: every entity observed in every period. The shipped county × year filing panel is 98.7% balanced: 3,092 of its 3,133 counties appear in all 11 years, 2006 to 2016.
Unbalanced: entities enter and exit. The raw UCC loan panel is heavily unbalanced: counties can have zero loans of a type in a year, firms borrow in some years only.
  • Unbalancedness is fine for FE estimation if exit is unrelated to the shocks (no selective attrition)
  • The paper's discipline: for county regressions it requires both a bank and a nonbank observation at the start and end (gen nm = loans_07_16!=. ; bys county: egen sum_nm = sum(nm); keep if sum_nm==2), and it winsorises growth rates at 1% (winsor2 loans_*_*, cuts(1 99)) so log changes from tiny counties do not dominate
  • Log changes such as ln(loans_2016) - ln(loans_2007) are only defined for positive counts: another source of (deliberate) unbalance

3 · The method

Fixed effects: the model and the problem

$$y_{ct} \;=\; \beta\,D_{ct} \;+\; \alpha_c \;+\; \delta_t \;+\; \varepsilon_{ct}$$
  • \(y_{ct}\): outcome for county \(c\) in year \(t\) (say, log small business loans); \(D_{ct}\): treatment (say, a branch closure has occurred)
  • \(\alpha_c\): a county fixed effect, everything permanent about the county (industry mix, remoteness, banking culture); \(\delta_t\): a year fixed effect, everything common to all counties in year \(t\) (the crisis itself)
  • The problem: if treatment is correlated with \(\alpha_c\) (closures happen in weak counties), pooled OLS of \(y\) on \(D\) is biased: \(\;\text{plim}\,\hat\beta_{OLS} = \beta + \dfrac{\operatorname{Cov}(D_{ct}, \alpha_c + \delta_t)}{\operatorname{Var}(D_{ct})}\)

In the paper the worry is the same in cross-section form: counties with a high 2006 bank share might differ permanently in ways that also affect nonbank lending: hence state FE, controls, pretrends and the within-firm estimator.

The within transformation

Averaging the model over time for each county and subtracting kills \(\alpha_c\):

$$y_{ct} - \bar y_c \;=\; \beta\,(D_{ct} - \bar D_c) \;+\; (\delta_t - \bar\delta) \;+\; (\varepsilon_{ct} - \bar\varepsilon_c)$$
  • OLS on the demeaned data is the within (FE) estimator: numerically identical to including a dummy for every county (LSDV), and to what xtreg, fe, reghdfe, fixest::feols and linearmodels' PanelOLS compute
  • Two-way FE: demean by county, then by year (iterate if unbalanced): removes both \(\alpha_c\) and \(\delta_t\)
  • Costs: FE only uses within-county variation, so anything time-invariant (including \(\alpha_c\) itself) is unidentifiable, and measurement error is amplified
  • What FE cannot fix: time-varying confounders that trend differently in treated counties: that is what event-study pretrends (simulation 3) are for

Clustered standard errors

County-level shocks make errors correlated within a county across years. OLS formulas assume independence, so they understate uncertainty. The cluster-robust (CRVE) estimator sums score contributions by cluster:

$$\widehat{\operatorname{Var}}(\hat\beta) \;=\; (X'X)^{-1}\Big(\sum_{g=1}^{G} X_g'\hat\varepsilon_g\hat\varepsilon_g' X_g\Big)(X'X)^{-1}$$
  • For a regressor that is constant within clusters and equicorrelated errors, the naive variance is too small by the Moulton factor \(\sqrt{1+(T-1)\rho}\): with \(T=10\) and \(\rho=0.5\) your t-statistics are about 2.3 times too big
  • Cluster where the shocks live: the paper clusters by county everywhere, and double-clusters by county and industry (or collateral) when the unit is county-industry (Table 5)
  • Consistency needs many clusters: with fewer than about 40 to 50, use small-sample corrections and treat p-values cautiously (Cameron and Miller 2015)

What makes this a good package (checklist for your own work)

  • One entry point (run_file.do) that reproduces everything in order: data creation first, then figures and tables
  • A README stating what runs what, where every raw dataset comes from, and who to contact (including how to buy the proprietary UCC data)
  • Folder discipline: rawdata/ → intermediate/ → final/, plus results/tables and logs/; scripts never overwrite raw data
  • Source comments: every import block starts with the download URL (census.gov for CBP and SUSB, bls.gov for LAUS, ffiec.gov for CRA, fdic.gov for deposits)
  • Tables straight to LaTeX with esttab: no hand-copied numbers, no transcription errors
  • Declared dependencies: user-written commands (reghdfe, winsor2, estout) are visible in the code, so you know what to install

5 · Simulation 1: the fixed-effects machine

A simulated county panel (10 years), a pure method demo: because here we know the true effect, you can watch each estimator hit or miss it. Branch closures target permanently weak counties: the confounding slider controls how strongly.

Bars are the three estimates of the closure effect; the dashed line is the truth. At zero confounding the three nearly agree; raise it and pooled OLS drifts away while the FE estimators stay put (two-way FE also strips the year shocks).
Reading the results. The gap between the pooled bar and the truth is omitted-variable bias: closures are not random, they happen in counties that were always weaker. Demeaning within county removes anything permanent, so the FE bars sit on the truth however hard you crank the confounding. This is why Gopal and Schnabl saturate their firm-level regressions with firm fixed effects (Table 6) and their county regressions with state FE and pretrend controls. A researcher reads "FE moved the coefficient a lot" as evidence that selection into treatment was real, and "FE barely moved it" (their Table 4, Panel A vs B) as evidence the result was not driven by that selection.

Simulation 2: the clustering machine

400 placebo panels: the regressor varies only between counties, the true effect is zero, errors share a within-county component ρ.

Naive OLS t-statistics across the 400 placebo panels vs the N(0,1) curve they should follow; red lines mark ±1.96. With ρ = 0 histogram and curve agree; raise ρ and the naive t-statistics fan out while clustered inference stays near 5%.
Reading the results. With ρ = 0.5 and T = 8, naive OLS rejects a true null about a third of the time: the Moulton factor √(1+(T-1)ρ) ≈ 2.1 says naive SEs are half their honest size, which is what the fanned-out histogram shows. The paper's bank share is exactly this kind of regressor and county shocks exactly this kind of error: hence county clustering in every table, double-clustering in Table 5, and caution below about 40 clusters (Cameron and Miller 2015).

Simulation 3: event study and parallel trends

Counties adopt treatment in different years, 2010 to 2013; the path shows the two-way FE effect by event time, relative to the year before treatment.

Dots are event-time coefficients (base t = -1) with ±2 SE whiskers. With the pre-trend slider at zero the pre-event dots hug zero, then the path drops by the effect size at t = 0 and stays down: a clean diff-in-diff. Add a pre-trend and the pre-event dots tilt: the post-event "effect" is contaminated.
Reading the results. Flat pre-event dots are the testable face of parallel trends. The paper's version: Table 3 shows near-identical 2002 to 2006 pretrends above and below the median bank share, and the firm-level year-by-year path (Appendix Table I.2) peaks at -0.032 in 2012 and vanishes by 2014, when nonbank substitution completes. If your pre-event dots tilt, no FE structure saves the design: find a better control group or model the trend.

Reading the results. The point of a robustness section is not to produce twenty identical numbers: it is to show which choices move the estimate and explain why. Here the specification ladder barely moves gamma (identification), while the clustering level only moves the standard error (inference): precisely the distinction the paper's Tables 4, 5 and I.4 to I.7 are organised around.

The real event study: timing is everything

Estimated on the shipped county × year panel: nonbank share on BankShare06 × year (base 2007), county + year fixed effects, county-clustered SEs. 2006 is excluded because the exposure measure is built from the 2006 counts.

Reading the results. The path is flat through 2009, then climbs every single year to +0.217 by 2016 and never turns back. Two lessons: the timing lines up with the bank shock (identification), and the reallocation is permanent rather than a crisis dip (economics). This is the real-data version of what simulation 3 teaches with a known truth: flat pre-event coefficients are what make the post-event path believable.

8 · Results and discussion

Connecting the dots

  • The replication: on the paper's own county data, this unit reproduces Table 4 exactly (0.212 / 0.534 / 0.016) and the crisis event study (flat to 2009, +0.217 by 2016), in your browser and in all four code files
  • Simulation 1 → Table 6: when treatment targets permanently different units, only FE (or a within-firm design) recovers the truth. The paper's firm-FE estimate (3.4 pp) is its cleanest supply-side evidence
  • Simulation 2 → every table: a county-level regressor plus county-level shocks means naive SEs are fiction; on the real regression the county-clustered SE is 27% larger than the naive one, and the paper double-clusters when industry or collateral shocks enter
  • Simulation 3 → the real event study: substitution took time and never reverted; flat pretrends (Table 3 and the 2008-09 coefficients) support the design
  • The economics: a permanent decline in bank lending (26% drop, never back to trend) with no real effects, because finance companies and FinTech lenders replaced the missing credit: by 2016 they made 59% of new small business loans

Using this in your dissertation

  • Design: county × year (or firm × year, bank × quarter) panels with an exposure-to-a-common-shock design are workhorse dissertations: define the exposure before the shock, show balance and pretrends, difference over the shock window
  • Estimation: two-way FE via reghdfe / feols / within-demeaning; cluster at the level of the exposure variable; check fewer-than-40-clusters problems
  • Data: CBP, LAUS, SUSB, CRA, HMDA and FDIC data are all free downloads: the paper shows how far you can get by merging public county-level sources onto one id
  • Craft: ship a run file, a README, raw/intermediate/final folders and esttab tables: your markers (and future you) will thank you
